### Various Election Issues in the Draft Constitution

— Our Newspaper Reporter Yu Zhenji

(3) Women and Overseas Chinese

According to Article 129 of the draft constitution, "Citizens of the Republic of China who have reached the age of 20 have the right to vote in accordance with the law. Except as otherwise provided in this constitution and laws, those who have reached the age of 23 have the right to be elected in accordance with the law." Chapter 2, Article 8 also stipulates that "Citizens of the Republic of China are equal under the law regardless of gender..." However, in this proposal, there are still some election-related issues related to women.

Representative Chen Guyuan and 62 others proposed adding a proviso to Article 130, "Candidates for various elections stipulated in this constitution shall openly campaign:" "However, regarding the election of representatives to the National Assembly and members of the Legislative and Supervisory Committees, the law should stipulate the minimum number of women elected." The reason is that in today's patriarchal society, men will surely win in open elections between men and women. Therefore, historically, we have always stipulated a minimum number of women elected to avoid the name of equality between men and women becoming an empty one. The British Parliament also has provisions for women. Representative Wei Tingsheng and 52 others proposed in Case 86 to add "representatives elected by women's organizations" under Article 26, "The National Assembly is organized by the following national representatives." They believe that this can prevent women from suffering losses when competing with men, and say that "women's problems must be solved by women."

The recently deceased female representative Liu Chunyi also proposed with Ni Guifu and 33 other representatives to add "the number of women among the various candidates stipulated in this party law shall not be less than 30%" after Article 130. This is more specific than the proposal of Chen Guyuan and others. As for why it is stipulated to be more than 30%, the original proposal did not explain.

Another election-related issue in this 100-odd proposal is whether overseas Chinese have the right to elect Supervisory Committee members. According to the provisions of Article 26, paragraph 1, item 2 and Article 95 of the draft constitution, overseas Chinese do not have the right to elect Supervisory Committee members. There are four proposals requesting supplementation submitted by Chen Shuren and others, for the following reasons: (1) Article 65 of the draft constitution stipulates the allocation of the number of Legislative Committee members, and includes "citizens residing abroad," but the allocation of the number of Supervisory Committee members lacks this item, so it is proposed to add it; (2) Overseas Chinese are frank and outspoken and are good at impeachment and supervision; (3) According to the provisions of Article 26, members of the Blue Committee and members of the Legislative Committee have the same qualifications, that is, they simultaneously have the qualifications of representatives to the National Assembly. Therefore, their method of generation should be the same as that of other representatives of the Legislative and Blue Committees, and they should be directly elected by the people. However, Article 26, paragraph 1, item 2 and Article 96 stipulate that the Supervisory Committee members are an exception and are indirectly elected by parliament. The nature of the differing laws should be uniform and the people should directly elect them.

In addition to the above-mentioned issues, the election issues involved in the proposal also include (1) direct elections vs. indirect elections, (2) whether the National Assembly representatives have the power to elect important officials other than the president and vice president, (3) whether incumbent officials can participate in the National Assembly representative election before being dismissed or approved, (4) the age of voters and candidates, (5) the election of National Assembly representatives in municipalities, etc. It is believed that there will be heated discussions on these issues in the review committee. ### The City's Budget for Next Year

The revenue listed in the city's budget for the 36th year is 210,193,981,125 yuan, and the total expenditure is 523,675,917,990 yuan, resulting in a deficit of 313,481,936,775 yuan. This deficit is already alarming. It is reported that the actual revenue figure, according to the city government's consultations, is only 190,800,000,000 yuan, in which items such as house tax and banquet tax are even estimated according to the current tax rates, not using the standards stipulated by the central government. Therefore, according to the most conservative estimate, the revenue is said to be only 110 billion yuan. In that case, the deficit would be more than 410 billion yuan, exceeding the actual revenue by about three times and exceeding the revenue agreed upon by the city government by more than double!

The city government's response is to adopt the principle of living within its means, which means calculating roughly double the budget for the second half of this year, totaling 214,017,433,470 yuan, and then considering further austerity measures to reach the assumed figure of 190.8 billion. The remaining new items that need to be handled will be budgeted separately and new sources of revenue will be sought separately. In other words, things will be done if there is money, and not done if there is no money; as much as can be done with the available funds. It is reported that the target for this new source of revenue is likely to be government bonds.

As the saying goes, "Even a skilled housewife cannot cook without rice." In reality, with only this much money, naturally only this many things can be done. The city government is not racking its brains to raise funds in every possible way, but is realistically maintaining the status quo and adhering to the principle of living within its means, which is largely worthy of sympathy.

Currently, local finances are all suffering from deficits. There are naturally many complex reasons for this, but the idea of carrying out all kinds of projects and trying to maintain appearances in many ways is the main cause of the deficits and their expansion. The reason for this is that the heads of various agencies, driven by a sense of responsibility, naturally want to do as much as possible in their areas of responsibility and harbor the ambition of doing everything at once. In addition, the instructions of superiors, the resolutions of long-term planning bodies, and the suggestions from various parties make it necessary to address all of them and proceed concurrently. As a result, important financial factors are relegated to secondary status. In order to reverse this trend and make up for the budget shortfall, there is no other way except for the highest officials of each ministry or locality to weigh the importance of things from time to time and in different places and make overall plans. For example, this time the revenue of Shanghai is limited to 190.8 billion, while the expenditure to maintain the status quo requires 214 billion. Judging from the figures, it seems that the income and expenditure are still insufficient. In fact, the budgets originally made by each unit (for the second half of the 35th year) were not very realistic. Originally, when preparing the budget and the plan for administration, they wanted to do as many things as possible and ask for as much money as possible. However, when the budget was finalized, many of the planned works could not be started due to various reasons, and as a result, many funds were frozen in the treasury and could not be withdrawn. Yesterday, a source told me that one-third of the expenses for a certain agency's projects had not been used. If this is reliable, then doubling the expenses for the second half of this year and using it as the expenditure budget for next year still needs to be discounted. Because although this is ostensibly maintaining the status quo, it still carries some idealism.

The Legislative Yuan has recently resolved separately that the tax rates for house tax and banquet tax are much lower than the current rates in this city. At a time when the city's finances are suffering a huge deficit, if we say that the taxes should be collected according to the central government's tax rates, it seems like we are joking with the city's financial authorities and being too unreasonable. In fact, this is a matter of the rule of law, not just a matter of money. If, after legally reducing taxes, there is indeed not enough revenue to cover expenses, our ideal is to rather find other legal new sources of revenue to compensate for the loss than to openly disregard the laws of the central government.

We must also admit that the difference these few sums of money makes is not enough to overly affect the city's budget. What will affect the city's budget the most in the future is prices, not a partial reduction of taxes. If prices continue to rise next year, and civil servants' salaries still need to be adjusted every three months as usual, and the cost of various construction materials, purchases, repairs, etc., must also be increased at any time, then the originally planned 5% contingency fund and the 36 billion in cost-of-living allowances will probably still be insufficient.

Therefore, in times of peace, the figures in the budget are indeed worth paying attention to. In these extraordinary economic times, it is more practical to stabilize prices and resolve the crisis in industry and commerce than to meticulously calculate the balance of revenue and expenditure. Looking at the city's revenue table, the business tax, license tax, and wharf tax are closely related to the prosperity or decline of the city, but other taxes such as slaughter tax, banquet and entertainment tax, clan guild tax, all kinds of fees, public business income, house tax, and construction tax attached to public utilities are also related to the city's well-being. Therefore, the accuracy of the city's budget for next year does not lie in the currently estimated figures, but in the changes in prices and the city's economic conditions.

### China and India (Part 2)

— (Written by Nehru)

Translated by Zhou Xiangguang, Our Correspondent in India

Xuanzang tells us a lot about the situation at Nalanda University, and other records are similar. A few years ago, I visited Nalanda University and was amazed to see many relics that had been systematically excavated from the ruins. Today, only a small portion has been excavated, and the rest is still inhabited by people, but these places still include vast gardens surrounded by solemn houses made of stone.

Shortly after Xuanzang passed away in China, another traveler, the monk Yijing, came to India. He set out from China in 671 AD and traveled for a full two years before reaching the port of Tamralipta in India, which is at the mouth of the Hooghly River. Because he came by sea, he stopped in Palembang (in Sumatra) and studied some months of Chuo Wen. This sea voyage was of great significance, perhaps due to changes in the political situation that made Central Asia unstable, making it impossible to come by land. Several Buddhist monasteries along the way were places for them to stop and rest. Sea routes may also be more suitable for the growth of Indian colonies in Indonesia and for establishing regular commercial relations between India and other countries. Records from Yijing and others show that at this time, there was regular navigation between Iran, India, Malaya, Sumatra, and China. Yijing first traveled from Guangdong to Sumatra by Persian ship.

Yijing also studied at Nalanda University for a long time and brought back thousands of Sanskrit classics. His main interest was in the key points of Buddhist rituals and rules, and he recorded these things in great detail. He also told us a lot about clothing, food, and customs. The main food in North India was wheat, as it is now, and in East India, rice was the main food. Meat was sometimes eaten, but it was rare. Ghee, milk, and other things could be seen everywhere, and there were many biscuits and fruits. Yijing recorded something extremely important for Indians, which is the reliance on the purity of a specific religious ritual. "Now the first and most important distinction between the five Indias and other countries is the sincerity of religious rituals. Moreover: 'Leftover food, which can be stored in China, may not conform to Indian regulations'."

Yijing once talked about India, saying that it is the West, but he himself tells us that India is called Aryadesha. The meaning of Arya is "struggle", and the meaning of Desha is "land", so it is the "land of struggle", all for the West. It is so named because the character of its people is noble and pure, and all the people praise this land with this name. India is also called "Central Land" (Madhyadesha) because it is located in the center of all the countries. All people feel close to this name. The northern barbarians (such as the Huns, Mongols, Turks) only call it Jingtǔ as India, but this is not a common word after all. This is just a local name and has no special meaning. The people of India do not know this name, and the most appropriate name for India is "Pure Land."

Yijing's discussion of Shendu is very interesting. He once said: "Some people say that the meaning of India (Indu) is the moon, and the Chinese call India as Yindu, which comes from this. Although its meaning may be like this, this is not a common name. Likewise, when Indians call China as Zhina, it's just a name and has no special meaning." He also mentioned the names used for Korea and other countries in Sanskrit.

Yijing has the utmost respect for India, and the same goes for Indian things. He described these things in detail and gave them to his motherland—China—first. India may be the "Pure Land," but China is the "Divine Land." "The people of the Five Indias are quite proud of their sincerity and nobility. But the reverence for thought, literacy, dignified demeanor, moderate attitude, welcoming etiquette, the deliciousness of food and soup, and the kind and just heart can only be obtained in China, not in other countries." The healing skills of acupuncture and the ingenuity of pulse detection cannot be replaced by any place in India, and the medicines that prolong life can only be seen in China... In terms of human character and the quality of things, China is named the Divine Land. Which of the Five Indias does not respect China?"

The ancient Sanskrit term for the Chinese emperor is Deva-Putra, which is accurately translated as the "Son of Heaven."

Yijing himself was a Sanskrit scholar. He praised Sanskrit and said that this language is also revered in the distant countries of the north and south. Many ancient Indian classics, not only those about Buddhism, but also those about Brahmanism, astronomy, mathematics, and medicine, are still preserved in Chinese and Tibetan. ### Ministry of Education Promulgates Regulations for Substitute National Schools; Municipal Education Bureau Ordered to Comply

In order to promote national education, the Ministry of Education has promulgated regulations for substitute national schools and ordered the education bureaus of various provinces and cities to comply. After receiving this instruction, the municipal education bureau will formulate specific measures to follow and implement. The Ministry's order promulgating the regulations for substitute national schools is excerpted below: (Article 1) These regulations are formulated in accordance with the provisions of Article 6, Paragraph 2 of the National School Law. (Article 2) In counties and cities where national schools have not yet been widely established, private primary schools with excellent performance, verified by county and city supervisors, and with the consent of the private primary school board of directors, shall be designated as substitute national schools, referred to as Substitute National School of Mou County (City), Mou Township, Mou Mou Bao. (Article 3) Substitute national schools are subordinate to the county (city) government or the education administrative organs directly under the jurisdiction of the municipality, and are subject to the management, supervision, and guidance of the competent authorities. However, the original board of directors of the school may still provide opinions, which shall be adopted and implemented by the competent authorities. (Article 4) After a private primary school is converted into a national school, its expenses shall be borne by the county (city) government or the municipal education administrative organs. However, the original property of the private primary school shall still be managed by the board of directors of the private primary school for the purpose of enriching school equipment, etc., and shall not be nationalized. (Article 5) After a private primary school is converted into a substitute national school, its principal shall be appointed by the county (city) government upon recommendation by the school board. Teachers shall still be hired by the principal. The original principal and teachers shall not be removed unless they are unqualified or there are circumstances specified in Article 12 of the National School Teachers and Staff Appointment and Treatment Protection Measures. (Article 6) Substitute national schools shall be divided into basic education, with four semesters of one to four years as a principle. When necessary, advanced (fifth and sixth grade) out-of-school public remedial education may also be established, with primary adult classes and women's classes as a principle. When necessary, advanced adult classes and women's classes may also be established. (Article 7) The curriculum, teaching materials, equipment, teachers and staff, and all other facilities of substitute national schools shall be handled in accordance with the rules of national schools and central national schools. (Article 8) After a private primary school is converted into a substitute national school, if the competent educational administrative organ intends to revoke its substitute status, or the board of directors of the private primary school requests to restore its private nature, it may restore its private nature after both parties agree. (Article 9) After a private school is converted into a substitute national school, if the original board of directors of the private primary school is unwilling to continue to operate and is willing to donate all the school's property to the public, its substitute nature may be revoked and it may be converted into a national school. (Article 10) These rules shall be implemented from the date of promulgation.

### Wuxi Industry Marches Forward Against the Headwinds of Economic Downturn

— Our Reporter Ding Weidong

Three Pillars: Cotton Mills, Flour Mills, Silk Mills

The degree of industrialization in Wuxi is second only to Shanghai on the Beijing-Shanghai Railway line. The number of industrial electricity consumption is sufficient to prove this fact. The power plant at Qi Qiao delivers 8,000 horsepower of power to Wuxi industry every day, which is not a small amount, but the shortage of electricity is still a problem. Large industries barely have enough power, and many small industries that sprang up during the war have to install their own power generation equipment at considerable cost. According to the engineers of the power plant, to meet all the needs of Wuxi industry, it would probably be necessary to increase the power by another 8,000 horsepower per day! And this is only the current situation. With the current high profits of cotton mills and the diligence and pragmatism of the people of Wuxi, there are quite a few people who are making expansion plans. Those who ordered spindles from overseas will continue to return in the next year or two. As productivity expands, the need for electricity will become even more urgent. If we use this as a measure, we can see that the future of Wuxi's industrialization is truly limitless!

There are three pillars of Wuxi industry: cotton mills, flour mills, and silk mills. Due to objective environmental factors, the prosperity and decline of these three industries are very different today. During my brief trip, I had contact with people from all three industries and gained a general understanding of the difficulties and prospects of each industry. Now, I will try to provide a comprehensive report in the order of cotton, flour, and silk.

### Lucky Cotton Industry: Profitable as Long as Management is Sound

The cotton spinning industry is undoubtedly the darling of heaven among China's troubled industries today. Demand is high while production is insufficient, the black market price is high, and raw materials are relatively cheap. Whether it is a large factory or a small factory, as long as the management is sound, there is no way not to make a lot of money. The price of cotton yarn in Wuxi is not limited, and the selling price is around 2.3 million yuan per bale. The prosperity of the yarn weaving industry can be imagined. As for the cotton mills in Wuxi, the larger ones with higher output include Shenxin No. 3 Factory and others. Please refer to the attached table below:

There are more than a dozen other small cotton mills with one or two thousand spindles. The above table lists the highest number of equipment in each factory. In fact, much was destroyed during the war, and some are still in the process of being repaired. Not so many are operating today. How many spindles are spinning in Wuxi today? There are two estimates: one is about 100,000 spindles, and the other is about 110,000 spindles. This means that the current yarn production is only about 60% of what it was before the war. At the beginning of the Anti-Japanese War, when the enemy occupied Wuxi, they deeply hated the Chinese textile industry, which hindered the national policy of "Industrial Japan, Agricultural China." 70,000 spindles of Shenxin No. 3 Factory were systematically destroyed, leaving only 10,000 spindles! As for the 1478 looms, they were completely destroyed. Shenxin's experience was like this, and the fate of other major factories was similar. But today, one year after the victory of the Anti-Japanese War, although the country is in trouble and the social economy is turbulent, I have been able to witness Shenxin being able to restore 50,000 spindles, and the weaving factory has begun to equip itself in the burnt-out factory building. It is possible that 500 looms will be running in December. Workers and staff are working with the same enthusiasm under the brilliant electric lights; other factories also have prospects and expectations for tomorrow. The past experience seems to be just a nightmare, and everything has disappeared with the victory. I deeply feel that the Chinese people's resilience and vitality are the strongest reliance on overcoming all difficulties. The raw materials for Wuxi yarn mills are mainly American cotton, and occasionally some Brazilian cotton. Before the war, they were able to produce fine yarns of 40s and 60s counts, but now the machinery is damaged, and they can only spin coarse yarns of 20s and 30s counts. The treatment of workers is also better than in other industries, generally ranging from 80,000 to 320,000, and the treatment of technical foremen can also reach 500,000 to 600,000 yuan. Their wages are calculated according to the cost of living. According to a friend in the factory, it is about 80% of the wages in Shanghai. Labor disputes in Wuxi are far less common than in Shanghai. There are two reasons: most of the major technicians in major yarn mills have been serving for many years, sharing hardships together. Over time, they gradually develop feelings. Even if there are any requests, they are made in a spirit of friendly consultation, and rarely appear in the form of strikes. Second, Wuxi is not like Shanghai. The local situation is simpler, and there is no large-scale organization or manipulation of workers, so the situation is more stable. Recently, Wuxi workers, in view of the high cost of living, have demanded that the base salary be increased by half. The management will take a rejecting attitude on this matter, so it is difficult to predict whether the labor-management relationship can be as it was in the past. As for the efficiency of technicians, one factory owner said that it is only 70% to 80% of what it was before the war. It is not physical weakness, but psychological changes. Technicians feel that they are welcomed everywhere, and "if you don't keep me here, I can find a place elsewhere." Therefore, discipline is lost, and efficiency is reduced. I usually feel deeply that, in terms of the current industrial situation in our country, except for the abnormally prosperous cotton spinning industry, the burden of wages will suffocate national industry. But today it is impossible to reduce wages. Therefore, the only remedy is how to improve workers' sense of responsibility and enable them to spontaneously improve work efficiency.

### Gloomy Flour Industry

Compared to the cotton yarn industry, flour mills and silk mills seem dim and lackluster. The latter is even in a crisis of raw material shortages. Before the war, flour was the main industry in Wuxi. The famous Rong family started their business with flour. The stone mill they used when they started their business is still on display as a family heirloom. During the nine years of the Anti-Japanese War, Wuxi experienced a distorted prosperity due to the enemy and puppet control of food. At that time, there were many people who raised funds to open flour mills. Today, the flour factories with scale in the area should be Maoxin, Guangfeng, and Zhenhua factories, etc. There are more than ten others that are smaller in scale. The current recession is mainly due to three reasons: (1) Insufficient power supply makes it impossible to fully exert productivity, but expenses cannot be saved. According to an estimate by a gentleman in this industry: The current daily output of flour in Wuxi is only 10,000 bags, but if there is enough electricity, it can be increased to 25,000 bags. This statement is quite reliable. In Maoxin No. 2 Factory, 8,000 bags of flour could be produced daily before the war, but now the output has been sharply reduced due to lack of electricity. 5,000 bags are produced on the first day, and 2,500 bags are produced on the second day, and so on. The situation in other factories is similar. (2) The supply of raw materials used to come mostly from northern Jiangsu, but due to the occupation by the Communist army, the supply is not smooth, which affects production. (3) The current general recession in the food industry, and the flour industry is no exception; the result is that the price of raw materials rises, while the selling price of flour does not rise, so there is no profit to be made. Under the above three unfavorable conditions, the current production of the flour industry is only 30% to 40% of what it was before the war. At the same time, the burden of taxation also shrinks the industry. Now, every bag of flour is subject to a goods tax of 400 yuan, and at the end of the year, there is also income tax and profit tax. A flour mill owner told the reporter that the monthly tax expenditure is much larger than the salaries and food expenses of the employees. Under the current situation of sluggish sales, small factories are deeply affected by high-interest loans, and one or two may announce liquidation at the end of the year.

The crisis in Wuxi's silk industry is even more serious than that of flour mills. Before the war, there were 40 silk factories in the area. During the nine years of the Anti-Japanese War, more than 20 small factories were established, for a total of more than 60 factories, employing more than 20,000 workers. When the autumn silk comes on the market next year, the cost will become a most serious problem. The current government purchase price is 3.8 million yuan per picul (standard silk), but the cost of autumn silk is estimated to be more than 6 million yuan per picul. ### A Productive City [More People Producing, Fewer People Consuming]

The current situation and prospects of Wuxi's three major industries have been briefly described above. Compared with other places, it brings hope. The same is true for industry and commerce. Of course, Wuxi's commerce is also in a recession, and there are also bankruptcies. On the day the reporter arrived in Wuxi, a large pharmacy was forced to close down by high-interest loans, and a rice store with more than ten years of history was struggling with a debt of 700 million yuan. The situation is miserable enough. However, the people of Wuxi who have money use their money to run businesses, instead of seeking high-interest loans and enjoying themselves, which has a very good impact. Because once a business is established, it employs a lot of people itself, and other industries also increase prosperity due to the so-called "multiplier effect" in economics. Therefore, the situation of unemployment in Wuxi is better than in other places. At the same time, because it is a productive place, the people's purchasing power is stronger, so even in the headwinds of the commercial recession, the market situation is still good. As for the lives of Wuxi farmers, they are not as good as they used to be. In the past, they had two sources of income every year, one was rice and the other was sericulture, and their lives were not bad. Since one year ago, they have been cutting mulberry trees and planting rice, but who knows that the rise in rice prices cannot be proportional to other prices! The hard work of farmers for a year cannot even make one or two sets of clothes. The big contradiction in our country's economy now is: if the price of rice is raised, the price of labor will definitely rise, and factories will not be able to maintain themselves; on the other hand, if the price of rice is not raised, then the lives of 80% of farmers will inevitably decline, and their purchasing power will be weak, and commerce will not be able to prosper. I asked this question to a friend in a Wuxi yarn mill. His opinion was: "In the past, the government tried to stabilize prices, but neglected labor prices. In the future, they should try to reduce labor prices in order to solve the problem."

Wuxi is a productive city, and there are very few idle people. There are "more people producing and fewer people consuming," so not only can it be self-sufficient, but it also has surplus material resources to export. The reason why it can be like this is because the natural environment is one reason, but the main reason is still human effort. The people's frugality and pragmatism, focusing on utilizing resources and improving people's lives, have finally turned Wuxi into a "Little Shanghai" - a productive city with the advantages of Shanghai but without the disadvantages of Shanghai. If three-tenths of the cities in China can concentrate the intelligence and wisdom of the people and develop into productive undertakings like the people of Wuxi, the reporter believes that the goal of improving people's lives can be realized sooner.

### Enterprising Spirit: Repair Wartime Wounds, Strive to Expand Business

The industrial community in Wuxi has a very strong enterprising spirit. Their greatest advantage is that they live frugally, do not engage in extravagance, and use all their talents to develop their businesses. Now, most factories have development plans. They not only want to repair the wartime wounds, but also strive to expand their businesses. Taking the yarn industry as an example, both large and small factories are placing orders overseas. For example, Shenxin Yarn Factory had 70,000 spindles before the war. It suffered heavy losses during the war and has now only recovered to 50,000 spindles. However, they are now planning to increase to 126,000 spindles! On the eve of the end of the war, they placed orders with American manufacturers, and it is estimated that a part of them will be shipped next spring. The other yarn mills ordered later, and there are currently only three factories in the United States that can manufacture spinning machines, and their own needs are also great. Therefore, the production of Wuxi yarn mills will not increase much within a year. Taking flour mills as an example, the temporary recession is not enough to make the enterprising industrialists feel discouraged. On the contrary, they have also set ambitious development plans. For example, Maoxin No. 2 Factory is planning to purchase machinery to establish a large factory in Wuxi that produces 10,000 bags of flour per day. At the same time, Maoxin No. 1 Factory is also adding new British-made flour milling machines that were used during the war. The product performance is particularly good. All of this shows their enterprising spirit.

### Power Problems

Insufficient power has restricted industrial production in the Wuxi area, and this problem is being smoothly resolved. There are various reasons for the power shortage in Wuxi. Firstly, many small industries have emerged in the past decade, and they all require electricity. Secondly, before the war, major factories had their own generators. However, during these turbulent years, some generators were lost, and some were left unused. This extra electricity burden affected the overall balance of supply and demand. As for the Qishuyan Power Plant's power supply, it has not decreased at all because the machinery was undamaged during the war. The Shenxin Textile Mill's generator is currently being borrowed by the power company to supply Wuxi. But this is still not enough. Many small factories cannot get electricity and have to generate their own power. Large factories also have limited electricity consumption; when exceeding the limit, they must supply coal to the Qishuyan Power Plant as compensation. Now, with the efforts of all parties, major textile mills will be able to resume their own power generation by next spring. The Qishuyan Power Plant will also receive a new generator and a wartime British generator, which will increase the electricity supply capacity. Therefore, the problem of insufficient power in Wuxi can be satisfactorily resolved in two or three months. In the past and present power allocation and dispatch, the Qishuyan Power Plant has indeed worked diligently and endured hardship.

### An Introduction to "A True History of the Taiping Heavenly Kingdom"

— Luo Hongtao

By Guo Tingyi

Published by the Commercial Press

Over 1400 pages

Price: 18,000 yuan

The Heavenly Capital of the Taiping Heavenly Kingdom fell to the Qing army on July 19, 1864 (corresponding to the 16th day of the sixth month of the third year of the Tongzhi reign). More than eighty years have passed since its demise, yet there is still no complete record of this great revolutionary movement, truly a great disgrace for historians. Now, the publication of Mr. Guo's book is just enough to make up for this major deficiency, truly a great blessing for the academic world. It is noted that people's views on the historical facts of the Taiping can be divided into four periods. From its demise onwards, the Qing court from top to bottom, as well as British and French imperialists, jointly engaged in malicious counter-propaganda, denigrating the Taiping army as even worse than bandits and brigands. At the same time, the populace fabricated various imagined cruel events, which marks the first period. Around the time of the founding of the Republic of China, the revolutionary atmosphere was already stirring, and the general public recognized the Taiping army as revolutionary martyrs. Consequently, various historical materials were fabricated (such as Mr. Liu Yazi's statement on August 20, the 23rd year of the Republic of China, that the original manuscript of Shi Dakai's poetry collection was compiled by his exiled friend Gao Tianmei. Much of it was fabricated, mostly by Tianmei, as propaganda for the revolution at the time, borrowing literature as a tool). Moreover, based on imagination, events such as Hong Xiuquan being a disciple of Zhu Jiu, Hong Daquan being named the Prince of Heavenly Virtue, and Qian Jiang submitting a memorial, appeared, creating seemingly true but false historical events, which marks the second period. In the ninth year of the Republic of China, Liu Niu Chenruo copied and printed sixteen interesting documents of the Taiping Heavenly Kingdom from the British Museum. From then on, more and more people began to unearth Taiping historical materials from overseas. Mr. Cheng Yansheng from the library of the School of Oriental Languages in Paris; Mr. Xiao Yishan from the Oriental Department of the British Museum in London; Mr. Wang Chongmin from the library of Cambridge University in England; and Mr. Yu Dawei from the Prussian State Library in Berlin, all had significant harvests. (According to scholars, among the four mentioned above, Mr. Xiao's acquisitions were the most numerous, while Mr. Yu's were the most refined. Mr. Yu's portion, proofread by Mr. Zhang Jusheng, was compiled into my humble edition of "Taiping Heavenly Kingdom Poetry and Prose Collection," but it is now out of print.) Furthermore, the Palace Museum in China also unearthed original historical materials, long hidden for a century, now reappearing to aid research, which is no small contribution. This marks the third period. However, although researching the historical facts of the Taiping became a fad among historians, what they obtained were only fragments. No one was able to conduct comprehensive research. That is, until Mr. Guo's book. In the preface, Mr. Luo Jialun states that Mr. Guo spent over a decade silently working on this issue before achieving this great harvest. Mr. Guo himself is modest, considering this book to be a historical record of tools, only seeking to prepare the way for others, hoping that historians with talent and scholarship can use it to create a complete record. This marks the fourth period. Objectively speaking, Mr. Guo's work is successful in the present situation.

This book's record of events begins in the first year of the Jiaqing reign and ends in the seventh year of the Tongzhi reign. In addition to specifically recording the history of the Taiping Heavenly Kingdom and the Nian Rebellion, it also describes the background of this great turmoil as much as possible, such as the turmoil during the Jiaqing and Daoguang reigns, peasant uprisings in various places, and the activities of the Tiandihui (Heaven and Earth Society) and the Sanhehui (Triads), all within the scope of the record. The recorded facts consider all sides, but due to limitations of materials, military battles account for the majority. The facts are recorded in chronological order. The months and days recorded include both the Chinese and Western calendars, and the new calendar implemented by the Taiping army is also noted where it can be verified. The facts of the months and days are strived to be accurate. For those involving doubts, various sources are consulted for textual research, truly worthy of being called an excellent work. At the same time, the author believes that local chronicles contain some important materials that cannot be found elsewhere, so they are used as much as possible. This is a new path opened for those studying Taiping history. Although he was able to borrow reference books from Tsinghua, Central, Yenching, and Jinling Universities, as well as the libraries of the Central Political, Beiping, and Nanjing National Studies institutions, and had good support, his spirit of research is also admirable.

The main text of this book is worth praising, and the eight appendices are also worth introducing.

The first appendix is a comparison table of the Taiping calendar and the lunar calendar, as well as a brief table of days of the week. Several people had done this work before Mr. Guo. However, the Heavenly Calendar's sexagenary cycle does not match the lunar calendar's sexagenary cycle, differing by one day. Also, the Taiping day of the week does not match the Western day of the week, also being one day earlier. Due to these two differences, the previous work was completely abandoned. Mr. Guo's table, however, is worth quoting and guaranteed to be error-free.

The second appendix is a table of figures from the Taiping Heavenly Kingdom, which is simply a small-scale biography of Taiping historical figures. It begins with explanations of titles and ranks, with detailed textual research. Each table is divided into items such as title, name, place of origin, experience, birth and death, and notes. The recorded events are rich, easy to check, and suitable for application.

The third appendix is a table of generals in major battles. Each battle records its main generals, subordinates, location, and opposing generals. There are a total of eighteen tables on the Taiping army side and seven tables on the Qing army side. These twenty-five tables cannot be written without having the entire historical facts organized in one's mind. What a rare effort.

The fourth appendix consists of fourteen maps of the wars between the Hong (Taiping) and Qing armies. "Left map, right history" was valued in ancient times. With these maps, reading history becomes even more interesting. Mr. Luo Jialun also states in his preface that the method of attaching maps comes from modern concepts. It would not be possible without the progress of modern technology.

The fifth appendix is a table of Qing ministers and officials. The sixth appendix is a table of commanders who suppressed the Nian Rebellion. Both tables record items such as office, name, term of office, original position, location of residence, and notes, which helps in checking. The seventh appendix is a brief record of generals from both the Hong and Qing sides, which is extensively collected and recorded.

The eighth appendix is a bibliography. The books listed are limited to those that Mr. Guo has referred to and used as a basis for compilation. The books are divided into two parts: one is Chinese, totaling 197 types, plus 380 types of local chronicles, and 8 types of periodicals; the other is Western, totaling 43 types. After each entry, a brief explanation and commentary are added to clarify its nature. There are over six hundred books cited, but what the author can say is that Mr. Guo's table is not to flaunt his collection, but Mr. Guo has actually cited them one by one. This is what makes it so valuable.

Finally, it must be stated that Mr. Guo's book is indispensable for those studying Taiping history. Mr. Xiao Yishan is a leading authority on Qing history and has also made many contributions to Taiping historiography, possessing the largest collection of Taiping historical books. Mr. Guo's book should have been read by him long ago. However, in recent correspondence, it is known that he still regrets not having seen this book. Even an expert is like this, let alone others. Therefore, not avoiding the risk of being meddlesome, I wrote this article to introduce it.

### Concerning the Soviets

— Jiuzhang

The author of this book, Mr. White, visited the Soviet Union in the summer of 1944 when the relationship between the United States and the Soviet Union was extremely harmonious. Especially during his debate with a female translator in Moscow, he frankly stated where he thought the Soviet Union was inferior to the United States; the frankness was probably surprising to the reader.

What I personally am particularly interested in is what is the life of the average Soviet person actually like? The author of this book happens to have this point of view: he does not vaguely discuss Soviet policies, but often wants to know the daily expenses of a worker. The standard of living of ordinary people is an achievement of governance and cannot be considered a military secret. In the third chapter, the author describes his visit to three Moscow factories: an aircraft factory, an automobile factory, and a biscuit factory. In the first factory, he inquired about working hours and wages. The working hours of ordinary workers are sixty-six hours per week, and the income is one thousand rubles. Eating a meal in the factory cafeteria costs five rubles a day, which is very cheap. But what can you buy with the remaining eight hundred or more rubles per month? In the "store," ham costs more than one thousand rubles per pound, and chicken costs around five hundred rubles per pound. Clothing is even more expensive. Therefore, this worker can fill his stomach by eating in the factory cafeteria and pass a few evenings by going to the designated theater, but "once he leaves the factory, his food and clothing immediately become a problem." He cannot save money like an American. Mr. White said, "The factory is theoretically owned by the workers, but in reality, the workers belong to the factory."

In the second factory, Mr. White asked the "manager" what happens if workers are lazy at work. "First, a warning is posted on the wall newspaper, and then a speech is given at the trade union." Mr. White immediately understood! The trade union has become an organization to restrain workers. Another time (Chapter 8), White asked a trade union leader, if a worker is dissatisfied with his current job, can he go somewhere else? The answer was, "He must apply to the factory." "If he leaves without permission, can he find work elsewhere?" The answer was, "No, he will have a very hard time finding work anywhere in the Soviet Union." Mr. White also asked, "Isn't this equivalent to a blacklist?" The answer was, "It can't be considered a blacklist." The "freedom" in the American mind cannot be found in the Soviet dictionary. As for the author's comparison of the lives of Soviet workers to the lives of American prisoners in Chapter 4, perhaps it is a bit too indignant. By analogy, "the treatment of workers throughout the Soviet Union is almost the same, because all factories belong to the government, and the treatment is all stipulated by the government." Some Chinese readers may agree with this approach, but Americans generally cannot accept it.

"In the Soviet Union, the lives of employed workers are not as good as the lives of workers receiving unemployment benefits during the economic panic in the United States." It is difficult to say how reliable this statement is. But the words of the Soviet female translator in the book can only be regarded as "self-deprecating." She said, "I am very miserable myself, but I know that everyone else is miserable, and I am comforted." Everyone with common sense knows that the slogan of socialism is to realize "everyone's paradise," not everyone being equally poor. There will always be a gap between the slogan and the reality. An ideal will always change somewhat when it is put into practice. This was the case before and after the French Revolution, and it is also true of the Russian Revolution.

The author has not studied the economic systems of the United States and the Soviet Union. He can only briefly talk about the shortcomings of the Soviet Union as perceived by Mr. White. (1) It is very difficult for the Soviet government to plan and guide the activities and development of factories across the country from one center (the Kremlin). The "managers" or responsible persons of each factory tend to be perfunctory and lack initiative, and some technical and management talents are sometimes purged due to political lines. (2) The elimination of the profit motive reduces competition, and people generally do not want to improve production methods. According to Mr. White, the American system has a greater incentive for new inventions than the Soviet system. The Stakhanovite movement was actually secretly restoring competition, but the encouragement of "medals" is not as good as actual benefits. "Generally speaking, capitalism invents, and socialism imitates." This is White's view.

### The Organization of Beggars 18

— Huang Jiajin

True and False Passengers

The local beggar gangs are stuck in the Shanghai market. Although they try hard to survive, they are ubiquitous, but they are always disheveled and haggard. It is not the ambition of a man. Therefore, the general young and strong people decided to find another way, which is in line with the original color of their "men's ambition is in all directions." These beggars, with lofty aspirations, actually went far away and began their travel career. This is the new route of the so-called "flying kite" in the beggar gang. However, this kind of livelihood still does not get rid of fraudulent methods. "Flying kite" means walking on boats. Shanghai has convenient transportation, and the water and land routes are as dense as cobwebs. The local beggar gangs can use the opportunity to travel to and from various places all year round without spending a penny. Moreover, every time they go out, they are sure to "tease the rake" (getting money). Without the real ability to stand alone, how can you build a family empty-handed?

Performance on the Boat

These people pretend to be passengers on the boat every day, and their clothes are quite neat, not revealing the true colors. When the passengers are complete and the boat leaves, they begin their crying and laughing skills. First, they greet the passengers next to them, and gradually talk about their own affairs. Then, they fabricate their past identity, such as being a businessman in their hometown, but unexpectedly suffered a disaster, the shop went bankrupt, and his wife died and his children were scattered. It seems that all the suffering in life is concentrated on one person. So the passengers on the boat gathered to ask questions, respectfully listening to his speech. Chinese people have always been honest and generous, especially when they have plenty of time on the journey and like to meddle in other people's business. Everyone meets by chance, and they feel particularly close. Soon, everyone spreads the word, and everyone can't help but feel pity. The beggar thinks that the good opportunity should not be missed, and asks the public for alms. At this time, everyone will generously donate money. In a short time, the collected number is considerable. When the ticket inspector comes, he finds that there is no ticket. The passengers will intercede for him and give him preferential treatment. Therefore, the beggar not only gets a free ride, but also returns fully loaded. The next day, he takes another ship back to Shanghai, and can repeat the tragedy again. After a long time, the crew will inevitably see through the trick, so they have to bribe them to silence them. Sometimes, they even need to distribute lubrication to be safe. But no matter what, as long as they travel a few times a month, the total income is far better than their peers on Zhengzhu Street.

This kind of "flying kite" drama is most common on ships traveling on the Yangtze River. The second is on sea vessels traveling to Ningbo Shaoxing. It turns out that the Shanghai Ningbo Shaoxing Association has regulations that poor fellow villagers who have fallen on Shanghai can be given a free boat ticket back home by the Association. The beggar pretends to be a fellow villager, but his goal is not the boat ticket, but to get a letter of introduction. The letter states that they can receive two yuan of ocean (the real situation before the war) from a certain place after returning home. And on the boat, they can rely on wonderful performances to get great benefits. Who would have thought that the Association's preferential treatment would instead create a slippery business for the beggars?

Station Drama

In addition to the "sea mountain" (ship) on the waterway, they also operate at the railway station. People in the gang call the train "flying centipede." But on the train, they can't ask passengers for help, because the feelings of passengers in the same car are very weak, not as cordial as the passengers on the boat who "share the same boat." Moreover, a fourth-class ticket is definitely not free. After they get off at their destination, they begin their crying and laughing performance when the passengers are crowded at the station. Asking the passengers for assistance. Several buses come and go at a station every day. Passengers are also constantly flowing, and the beggar can be active at Station A one day, and then take the bus to Station B the next day. Although they are dusty and tired, the income is considerable. This is the new route for the "flying kite" gang to leave the dock.
