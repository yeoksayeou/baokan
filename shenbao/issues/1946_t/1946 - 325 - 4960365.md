### Editorial: Establishing a New National Ethos

Since the Legislative Yuan temporarily suspended the review of the draft constitutional amendment due to procedural issues, all parties have paid great attention to the constitutional draft issue. The drafting of the constitutional amendment was originally based on the resolutions of the Political Consultative Conference (PCC). Whether this amendment will be submitted to the National Assembly is not only related to the credibility of the government but also to the future of the overall situation. For this reason, this newspaper has previously commented on the constitutional draft issue, believing that the drafting of a Three Principles of the People constitution has no precedent to follow, and everything is a pioneering effort. The drafting of the May 5th Constitution took great effort and fully reflected the spirit of the Three Principles of the People. However, whether the system it established is the most reasonable in light of the evolution of the past decade, especially in recent years, is also questionable. As for the amended constitutional draft that the government plans to submit to the National Assembly, it still adheres to the solemn stance of the Three Principles of the People while complying with the resolutions of the PCC and should serve as the basis for the National Assembly to formulate the constitution. As for the procedural issues, as long as the Central Standing Committee makes a resolution, it can be made compliant with the regulations in a timely manner.

Now, the Central Standing Committee has indeed passed the constitutional amendment and handed it over to the Legislative Yuan to quickly complete the legislative procedure. At the same time, it also passed the National Government Organization Law Amendment. The Supreme National Defense Council also passed the Executive Yuan Organization Law Amendment. The contents of each proposal all comply with the spirit of the PCC resolutions. The passage of this motion can, on the one hand, enable the Legislative Yuan to review the constitutional amendment in accordance with the legal procedures, complete the review in a timely manner, and submit it to the National Assembly. On the other hand, it shows the people of the country how the government will abide by its promises and resolutely adopt the resolutions passed by the PCC. All absurd talk can be disproved by the Central Standing Committee's resolution this time. The government values its own promises and will not easily allow them to be changed. Unless it is outside of the fundamental stance, the central government's impartiality should indeed move people from all parties and factions, even those without any affiliation. The outdated notion that "only by believing in our doctrine can you be our citizen" may still linger in the minds of some people, but it can never represent a political group that is truly dedicated to serving the public. Only those political groups that outwardly advocate democracy but actually have no individual freedom and worship collectivism have such a terrible notion.

The National Government Organization Law Amendment passed by the Central Standing Committee stipulates that in addition to the Chairman of the National Government being elected by the Central Standing Committee, there will be forty members of the National Government Committee, including people from both within and outside the party, and the power of the National Government Committee will be expanded. At the same time, the Executive Yuan Organization Law Amendment passed by the Supreme National Defense Council stipulates that in addition to changing the Ministry of Transportation to the Ministry of Transportation and the Ministry of Posts and Telecommunications, three new ministries will be established: the Ministry of Land Administration, the Ministry of Water Resources, and the Ministry of Health. There will also be political affairs commissioners. All these resolutions follow the spirit of the PCC resolutions, which shows the government's determination to comply with and implement the PCC resolutions. Due to the passage of this amendment, the reorganization of the National Government and the Executive Yuan to accommodate people from all parties and factions, even those without any affiliation, will be accelerated. We hope that all parties can further cooperate sincerely to make the organization of the National Government and the Executive Yuan more substantial, so as to establish a new model of democratic unity. The future of the nation will thus gain more hope.

Although the news from China is unfavorable, the British public's opinion of China is not bad. The London Times publishes news about China every day, and publishes an editorial on China every month or two. Every year on Double Ten Day, the Times publishes an article analyzing the Chinese situation. The Times's position is fair, and it often criticizes the CCP's illegal and reckless behavior. Other London newspapers rarely publish Chinese news. Occasionally, they publish one or two articles, but 19 out of 20 are in the style of tabloids or evening newspapers. For example, what Chinese people eat, what they wear, and photos of young ladies wearing qipaos often appear in the gossip columns or miscellaneous sections of various newspapers. The News Chronicle used to publish many articles about China because of Geld, who promoted the CCP. However, he himself did not sympathize with the CCP. In fact, it was because he had disagreements with several spokespersons in Chongqing, and as a result, he developed a dislike for the entire Chinese government. When his dislike reached its extreme, he actually sided with the CCP. The Chronicle's view of China has gradually been corrected. Their political commentator, Mr. Bartley, is about to visit China. Mr. Bartley's world political vision has always been admired by the British. His view of the September 18th Incident is completely consistent with China's view. The Manchester Guardian, which has always been considered by the Chinese to sympathize with China, publishes very little news about China. Occasionally, there is an editorial, but its tone does not show "sympathy."

British magazines have mixed effects on China. The "Economist," the most influential weekly magazine, often has articles on China. Regarding China's inflation, it bluntly pointed out that the civil war is the root of all evils. This time, the leftist weekly magazine "New Statesman and Nation" has a tone about China that is somewhat similar to "Xinhua" and "Liberation." Its position is well known, just like the Daily Worker (organ of the British Communist Party), whose views are not taken seriously. The most successful point of the CCP's external propaganda is their so-called "peasant movement," which boasts of its rent reduction and other land reform policies in the border areas. Most British newspapers, including The Times, believe that the CCP has a solution to this urgent social problem.

The British are generally not concerned about the Hong Kong issue. Some people are very willing to return this island to China. Those who oppose it are still the "old China hands." What the people in Britain understand the least is: why do the Chinese in Hong Kong have such a deep dislike for the British? This suspicion is very natural, because the British in England do not know the life attitudes of the British in colonies, semi-colonies, and quasi-colonies - the arrogance of imperialists. These imperialists swagger and dominate abroad, but when they return home, their attitude changes and they are the same as ordinary, humorous British people.

The British do not understand China enough. Our country's foreign propaganda authorities should pay attention, but relying on propaganda alone is not enough. To make a nation understand us, introducing cultural life is one way; but clarifying the current domestic situation is the first thing. Everyone knows that China has a long history and a great culture, but if what is revealed from this great cultural background is all bad news, the big question mark in foreigners' minds will never be eliminated. Recently, in order to encourage exports and fully supply funds, the Four Joint Offices have approved the export material loan, re-pledge, and re-discount measures proposed by the Central Bank and notified various banks and bureaus to handle them. The measures are as follows:

(1) Participating Banks: The Three Banks and Two Bureaus and various foreign exchange designated banks are limited to applying for this re-pledge and re-discount.

(2) Loan Targets: Exporters who directly operate their own business and join the local industry association are limited to those who need financial accommodation to purchase export materials in the mainland or who request to handle discount loans for export materials in transit.

(3) Term: The original pledge shall not exceed 90 days. The term of the discount loan shall be determined according to the transportation situation. (4) Limit: The original pledge loan shall not exceed 200 million yuan per household. The discount loan shall not exceed 300 million yuan per household. Those approved by the Four Joint Offices on a special case basis are not subject to this restriction.

(5) Collateral and Discount: The collateral is limited to the exporter's own export materials, and the discount is calculated at 70% of the cost.

(6) Interest: For the purpose of encouraging the export of materials, the original loan interest rate for this pledge discount loan shall not exceed 3% per month, and no other fees shall be charged. (8) Re-Discount Discount: The re-pledge re-discount discount is calculated at 90% of the original loan.

### "Collapse Wind" Strikes Hangzhou

— Special Correspondent Chu Yusheng

Hangzhou, 10th

After the victory, the complex and intricate nature of the takeover institutions caused unrest in domestic industry and commerce. Unexpectedly, domestic wars arose one after another, pushing this already fragile industry and commerce into a difficult predicament. It has now reached its most dangerous eve. In major cities across the country, the sound of merchant houses and firms collapsing is frightening and chilling. This is clearly bordering on the verge of industrial and commercial collapse. If no measures are taken to provide relief, then it will undoubtedly wither and die!

Collapse Wind Spreads Across All Industries

Hangzhou was originally a city that exported special products, such as silk, tea, and tung oil. In previous years, it not only won wealth for Zhejiang Province through the export of these special products, but also won back considerable honor in the international market. However, the export of special products after the war has become an extremely serious problem. Foreign markets already have substitutes or identical finished products. Domestic markets are plagued by chaos everywhere, coupled with currency devaluation, high costs, dumping of foreign goods, heavy profiteering, and onerous taxes, causing special products to encounter serious obstacles everywhere. As a result, they went from struggling to maintain to being unable to maintain. The number of merchant houses and firms that went from being unable to maintain to collapsing and being destroyed is indeed a considerable figure. This collapse wind has spread to every industry, with tea firms being the most affected. In thirty-six days, twenty-eight have already declared closure. Although there are no accurate statistics on other closed shops for the time being, there were twenty-four business units that applied to the city government for record and closure procedures in October, such as five in the cloth industry, five in the entertainment industry, one in the grain industry, one in the electrical machinery and silk weaving industry, two in the laundry and dyeing industry, one in the silk business, eight in the coarse paper industry, one in the sundry goods business, one in the preserved meat business, one in the native tobacco business, one in the timber rafting business, and one in the cotton weaving business. If we use monetary figures to illustrate its severity, it will be over 200,000,000,000 yuan.

Reasons for Tea Firm Closures

According to the operators, the reason for the tea firm closures is the government's levy of tea commodity tax. In its heyday, Zhejiang tea was originally subject to a special product tax levied by the province. Later, the provincial government abolished the special product tax in an attempt to revive the tea industry. Not long after, the Commodity Tax Bureau levied commodity tax again. When tea leaves were facing extreme difficulties with no way to export, they could not withstand the collection of this kind of tax, so they closed down one after another. Another reason is the exploitation of high-interest loans. Tea firm owners used funds obtained from high-interest loans to operate the tea business, which did not yield very high profits, and naturally felt somewhat strained. Conversely, if they used the funds for running a tea firm to make high-interest loans, they could worry much less. Therefore, the tea industry fears that it will not be able to restore its inherent prosperity for a while. The result of these tea firm closures will be that tea farmers will have fewer outlets for their tea leaves, and tea farmers will suffer extra blows and hardships. On the other hand, this nearly 100,000,000,000 yuan in funds will turn into floating capital, speculating and causing trouble in society.

In addition to the severe exploitation of high-interest loans and the two-pronged attack of the enticing allure of speculation causing closures of stores, another reason for the closure of many other firms is the decline in social purchasing power. The income of civil servants for a month is not enough to eat and dress warmly, let alone have the surplus to buy new things. The countryside is bankrupt, factories are precarious, and fellow farmers and workers also lack the ability to purchase. Moreover, due to the temptation of high-interest loans, even the remaining purchasing power that has been forgotten and left behind is transformed into floating capital, joining the ranks of high-interest loan businesses. Some of those who have obtained wealth from high-interest loans want to accumulate more and more, while others, although they buy some things, are ultimately a small minority. Therefore, in the market of Hangzhou City, many shops are seen every day decorating with lanterns and colored streamers, blowing and beating instruments, hanging large bargain flags with red silk and gold lettering, holding great sales from spring to summer, and then to autumn and winter throughout the year, but business is still far less lively than usual in previous years. If these great sales still do not make money, then the only option is to close down. The number of shops closing down in this way will increase more and more, the floating capital in society will accumulate more and more, and the production units will collapse more and more. Meanwhile, foreign goods are coming in continuously, both cheap and ingenious. It is unthinkable that our Chinese products can be sold abroad again, and because of the cheap and fine quality of foreign goods and the unrest in the domestic situation, it is also unthinkable that they can be sold to various parts of our country.

Some say that to rescue China's current industry and commerce, only government banks can provide large amounts of low-interest loans. This goal is precisely the beautiful aspiration that the various business communities in Hangzhou are unanimously pursuing. However, if the government provides loans, the bright future after obtaining the loans is highly questionable. For example, when purchasing silkworm cocoons at high prices, the government sets a considerable price, but cocoon merchants still clamor that it is not high enough, while in reality, the purchase price is often less than the government-set price. The reason for this is because the bank has loans, and the government lends to the cocoon merchants according to the set price. The cocoon merchants use a small amount of money to buy cocoons, and use the other money for other purposes. - These purposes are presumably for things like high-interest lending. Therefore, in order to ensure that these loans are indeed used in the industrial and commercial sectors, and that they are indeed for seeking industrial and commercial revival, there must be strict investigation, meticulous lending, and after lending, there must also be thorough assessment. Some people say, if these funds are lent out, won't inflation increase further and the currency depreciate further? I believe that as long as these many loans are directly lent to production units, and truly make one yuan produce the effect of one yuan, then it can still revive a portion of industry and commerce.

Grand Old Slogans

In addition, we would like to propose a grand old slogan that has been ineffective for decades, which is "Chinese people should use Chinese goods." First, we must cultivate a national mentality of loving domestic goods. I remember that before the war, in the Hongkou area of Shanghai, there was a Japanese child who wanted to buy a box of cigarette lighters. He went to a Chinese shop, and the shop assistant told him: "We have Chinese lighters, which are cheaper and better than Japanese ones. Why don't you buy a box to try!" But the child shook his head and left. He still went to a Japanese shop to buy a Japanese lighter to take back. This story can alert us, can awaken us. Encouraging the Export of Special Products

Therefore, the government should invest heavily and reluctantly encourage the export of special products, even at a loss. For example, the tea firms and shops have closed down as many as twenty-eight due to the increase in commodity tax, which is worth the government's consideration.

Need for Expert Research

We may also invite experts to conduct detailed research on these many special products and invent various other daily necessities, just like Germany during the war, which was able to turn [illegible] into bread substitutes, and the United States after the war, which was able to make socks, leather shoes, leather bags, etc. from plastic (Plaztic), which can be said to be the result of research.

### The Re-education of Over Two Million Taiwanese Compatriots - Civic Training in Taiwan

— He Minxian

Taiwan Air Mail, 12th

On this National Day of historically great significance, on the treasure island of Taiwan, is the first day that 2,394,685 male and female youth citizens successively began to receive "re-education". Regarding the unprecedented training work of this large-scale and universal nature held in the province, it can be regarded as unprecedented and truly an initiative. According to the relevant authorities, the training motivation, goals, and the content and process of the work carried out are as follows:

Understanding the Actual Situation of the Country

The motivation for the Taiwan Provincial Government to re-educate these two million citizens is based on the fact that Taiwan Province was under Japanese rule for fifty-one years in the long years of shackles. Japan has consistently implemented a policy of isolation, gradually alienating compatriots in this province from the motherland. Now that the province has been restored for almost a year, but none of us can deny the necessity for compatriots in this province to have a deep understanding of everything about the motherland. The greatest purpose of this event is to exercise civil rights and hope to build a sound foundation for our local autonomy. Then, following the instructions of Chief Executive Chen, adult training should be held quickly, using a simple and clear method to tell them about the history, geography, and customs of the country, so that they can understand the actual situation of the country. Therefore, the "Taiwan Provincial Civic Training Committee" was organized to be responsible for hosting and promoting everything. Because the educational level and law-abiding habits of compatriots in this province are higher than any other province in the mainland, as long as they have a deep understanding of everything about the motherland in the future, they will definitely make unparalleled contributions to the country and the nation!

Six Training Objectives

Its training objectives are six: (1) Strengthen the understanding of the Chinese nation, (2) Deeply understand the current situation of our country, (3) Actively promote the essence of the Three Principles of the People, (4) Fully develop the virtues of loyalty, filial piety, benevolence, love, faith, righteousness, and peace, (5) Comprehensively and universally promote government decrees, (6) Train popular teaching.

Three Basic Organizational Layers

The basic organization for promoting this training work is divided into three layers of agencies: provincial, county (city), and township (town). Such as (1) At the provincial level: organize a civic training committee, with the civil affairs office, education office, propaganda committee, and other relevant personnel as members, to preside over civic training matters throughout the province, and with Director Zhou Yi'e of the civil affairs office as the chairman; (2) At the county and city levels: also establish a committee with the county mayor as the chairman; (3) At the district and township (town) levels: assign the district chief and township chief as supervisors.

Two Training Methods

As for the training methods, according to their nature, they are divided into classroom-style training, that is, setting up "civic training lecture halls," and the general extensive training, that is, "social civic training." (A) Civic training lecture halls - using local schools, churches, Sun Yat-sen halls, and other public places in each village and neighborhood as the hall site. (If there is a lack of teachers, one lecture hall may be set up jointly by two to three villages and neighborhoods. In the first phase, each household designates one male or female person aged 20 to 30 to participate. If the venue permits and there is no difficulty in recruiting teachers, two classes for men and women can be set up separately. The training period for each phase is set for one month, two hours each day, with the time arranged so as not to hinder their work. (B) Social civic training - using local cinemas and theaters as lecture halls for implementing social civic training, broadcasting slides and lectures for 30 minutes before each screening, or using other methods that arouse their interest. At the same time, broadcasting civic training courses on the radio for 30 minutes every day.

### A General Discussion of Chinese Historiography

— Written by Mu

Author: Zhu Xizu Publisher: Independent Publishing House

A general understanding of the various systems of Chinese historiography is essential for every friend who loves history. Young people, in general, must understand the developmental process of history, and they should also pay attention to the development of historiography. This is because historical records are not necessarily reliable. Throughout history, most have been "official histories" based on imperial chronicles, and these records inevitably contain inaccuracies. Understanding the development of historiography allows one to distinguish between truth and falsehood, providing a great deal of help in reading history.

This book, "A General Discussion of Chinese Historiography," is based on the author's lecture notes from the Department of History at Peking University, compiled by his son-in-law, Luo Xianglin. It provides us with a concise overview of Chinese historiography, allowing us to understand the development of Chinese historiography throughout history.

First, the author discusses the origin of Chinese historiography. Regarding the basic meaning of the word "史" (history), the "Shuowen Jiezi" (Explaining and Analyzing Characters) says, "史 is one who records events. It follows '又' (hand) holding '中' (middle), meaning impartiality." The author provides a brief explanation and examples of its use. He then explains that history includes written records, records kept by scribes, falsely attributed records, and history inferred from ancient artifacts before the advent of writing. He discusses the advantages and disadvantages of these various historical systems, which were not called "history" in the old days. The "history" of that time was merely the not-very-accurate "official history" produced by scribes, which gradually developed and nurtured the seeds of history.

Regarding the schools of Chinese historiography, the author discusses six schools, spanning from the Spring and Autumn period to the present day. These six schools are as follows:

(1) Annals: Distinguished by time, also known as chronological history. This type of history began after the Western Zhou dynasty. The era before the Western Zhou is unclear, so this system did not exist before that time. Common examples of this type of history include comprehensive works such as the "Zizhi Tongjian" (Comprehensive Mirror to Aid in Government) and individual works such as the "Spring and Autumn Annals."

(2) National Histories: Distinguished by place, also known as local history. This type of history collects and examines the history of various ancient states. The ancient states, both large and small, cannot be simplified. Comprehensive examples include the "Records of the Three Kingdoms," and individual examples include the "Chronicles of Huayang."

(3) Biographies: Distinguished by people. Within these biographies, "one can glimpse the level of civilization in a place and the gains and losses of society." Common examples include "Biographies of Women" and "Unofficial Biography of Dongfang Shuo."

(4) Political History and Cultural History: Distinguished by subject. Political history includes economic history, legal history, military and political history, social party history, and diplomatic history. Cultural history includes academics, religion, literature, art, agriculture, industry, commerce, customs, and other items. Examples of the former include the "Tongdian" (Comprehensive Statutes) and "Tongkao" (Comprehensive Examination of Institutions), and the "Tang Liudian" (Six Statutes of the Tang Dynasty). Examples of the latter include "Bielu" (Separate Records), "Qilue" (Seven Summaries), "Song Yuan Xue'an" (Case Studies of Song and Yuan Scholars), and "Poetry Criticism," etc.

(5) Official History: The so-called official history is not written by one person at one time, but is a "official history" accumulated over generations, such as the Twenty-Four Histories.

(6) Chronicle of Events from Beginning to End: Histories written about the beginning and end of an event. This system was created by Yuan Shu of the Song Dynasty. Examples include "Chronicle of Events from Beginning to End of the Comprehensive Mirror," "Chronicle of Events from Beginning to End of the Three Feudatories," and "Chronicle of Events from Beginning to End of Western Xia."

The above six types are only a brief outline. The book discusses them in detail in separate sections.

There are two appendices. One explains "Taishigong Jie" (Understanding the Grand Historian), elaborating on Sima Qian's writing of history. It is best to refer to Jian Bozan's "Historical Materials and Historiography," specifically the article "Sima Qian's Historiography," for a more detailed understanding. The other appendix is an examination of the compilation of annals for the twelve emperors of the Han Dynasty, from Emperor Gaozu of Han, through Emperor Hui, Empress Lü, Emperor Wen, Emperor Jing, Emperor Wu, Emperor Zhao, Emperor Xuan, Emperor Yuan, Emperor Cheng, Emperor Ai to Emperor Ping. The author describes and lists these twelve emperors' annals, providing a clear and precise indication of the same.

Although this book is concise, its content is quite extensive. Young friends who are just beginning to study history can use it as a good reference. Even if there are omissions, they can still be found, and at this time, it can still serve as a reference for general beginners. The book also points out many errors in historiography.

Today, everyone knows that history is an important discipline in the social sciences. It can't be ignored by every young person. Before reading history, we must understand the various branches of Chinese historiography, so that we can get a lot of convenience when reading history.

This book can serve as a preparation before reading history, and it can also serve as a reference after studying history.
