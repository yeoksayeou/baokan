Recent Situation of the League of Nations 1

Geneva, 5th - A dispatch reports that today's meeting of the League of Nations, though quite激昂(stirring/intense), saw representatives of various small nations speaking at length, strongly advocating for the upholding of the dignity of the League of Nations charter. However, the outcome still awaits the adjourned meeting on the 7th. This adjourned meeting on the 7th is bound to feature even more significant debates, and whether the League of Nations charter can be effectively upheld and whether the Sino-Japanese issue can be appropriately resolved will depend on the outcome of this meeting. During the meeting on the 5th, the three proposals put forward by the Spanish representative were the most crucial: (1) The responsibility of the assembly lies in declaring that the charter requires the prior withdrawal of troops before negotiations. (2) The assembly must not allow people to forget that Article 10 of the charter is the highest constitution of the League of Nations. (3) The League of Nations charter should be made particularly forceful to assist smaller nations. Otherwise, it will be impossible to resolve the dispute between China and Japan, maintain the standing of the League of Nations itself, and uphold the dignity of the Kellogg-Briand Pact and the Nine-Power Treaty. Furthermore, it will be impossible to solidify the foundation of the disarmament conference. Consider the commentary in today's British Review's editorial by史梯德(Stead), chief editor of the Review of Reviews, published in The Sunday Times discussing the Far Eastern situation. It states that the United States has recently offered a hint: if Japan continues to violate the Kellogg-Briand Pact and the Nine-Power Treaty, the United States will definitely undertake large-scale defense projects in the Philippines and Guam. If such a development occurs, the United States Navy will undoubtedly increase significantly, and Britain will be forced to expand its navy in the same way, unless Britain is willing to abandon the principle of Anglo-American naval equality.
