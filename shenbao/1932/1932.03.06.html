New Resolutions of the League of Nations 1

After handling the dispute between China and Japan, the League of Nations had established three resolutions. The League itself took no action on the first resolution, nor did it put forward any concrete suggestions, so it is not worth discussing. The second resolution clearly stipulated the final deadline for the Japanese withdrawal, but was "tabled" (Briand's words) due to Japan's opposition vote. The third resolution's six articles were the only achievements of the League's months of hard work, but although it was full of shortcomings and also empty of content, the result was that even this empty resolution became ineffective. The Shanghai incident intensified the situation in the Far East. A major war has been raging for a month, and the League of Nations now has this fourth Geneva resolution. The past three resolutions have already exposed the League's lack of capacity, and this resolution more clearly shows its lack of sincerity in resolving the dispute.

If we try to examine the content of the League's three resolutions this time, we can know that first, the League has misjudged the nature of the Shanghai incident, that is, forgetting that the Japanese army was defending itself by attacking China. Second, the League only hopes for peace and asks for peace, but forgets to identify who is the culprit for destroying peace, especially blurring the line between peace that conforms to justice and peace that goes against justice. Peace should be strived for according to justice, and should never be willing to sacrifice anything in order to achieve peace and sacrifice justice for peace. Third, the League has no determination to sanction violence. Article 16 of the Covenant clearly stipulates economic sanctions, but the League has regarded it as a dead letter and has never dared to adopt it. Fourth, the League itself has no ability to resolve disputes, and it does not dare to stand on the side of facts and resolutely take responsibility, but shirks responsibility to neutral countries to mediate. Fifth, the League actually trusts the Japanese spokesman's claim that military operations have ceased, and ignores that the Japanese army is still continuing to attack. We have already heard the League's four resolutions and cannot help but feel disappointed and pessimistic. We believe that the League has never had the ability or sincerity to resolve disputes from beginning to end. The expansion of the Northeast incident and the outbreak of the Shanghai incident can almost be said to be half due to the Japanese aggressors, half due to our government's absolute trust in the League of Nations, failure to seek self-help, and the League's indecisiveness, cowardice, and neglect of its responsibilities.

From this we can know that the last efforts that our people and the world have hoped for from the League of Nations in the past six months are now almost hopeless. In order to maintain the survival of our nation and strive for justice and righteousness, the only way left is to rely on self-reliance, self-help, and self-salvation.
