國聯調查團成功之先例翰·

日前李頓爵士應外次郭泰祺之宴。席次贈言。謂「國聯成立已届十年。遭遇危機已非一次。莫不設法消弭。從未失敗。念已往之成績。深信今茲之事件。必得圓滿之解决。」聊聊數語。其自信力與責任心溢於言表。我人除希望其克盡責任完成自信以外。更有何說。爵士所謂已往之成績。以我人思之。其最著者殆惟一九二五年之布希糾紛一事乎。

考一九二五年十月二十二日。希臘軍大舉犯布加里亞。佔布國之邊境。布政府馳電日内瓦。要求召集行政會。引用盟約第十條及第十一條。行政院開會後第一步工作。即在設法停止敵對行爲。第二步工作。勒令希臘撤兵。結查皆如國聯所願。是時主席白里安居間斡旋。尤爲努力。痛斥一般不守盟約之國家。供自衛之名。行侵略之實。行政會且决議組織一調查團。任務約分三點。第一調查事實。判明責任。第二規定賠償。第三建議解决糾紛方法。白里安以。此一事件。得循國聯正當之徑。進行順手。至以爲報而英外長張伯倫。更認此一事件。足爲任何會員國之楷模。當時首先贊成張伯倫之發言者。日本代表安達是也。

調查團工作之結果。確定希臘侵占布國之土地。實係違反盟約。任何託辭。皆不足掩飾此侵略之事實。而賠償之責。亦即由希臘任之。統觀全案。自始至終。依據盟約。迅速解决。而調查團之工作。實與有力。當時調查團工作完竣。行政會特組織一審查委員會。委員長適爲日本代表安達。其瀋查報告。尤義正詞嚴。曰「侵犯鄰國之土地。雖爲種環境所逼迫。亦决不能掩飾違約之事實。而圖卸賠償責任。我人認此爲最高之原則。必得行政院全體之贊同。」　　此誠國聯解决糾紛案中最光榮之一頁也。是時非特白里安輩堅持正義。不可撓屈。即日代表亦知國聯約章之尊嚴。有儼然不可侵犯之色。然返觀今日中日之糾紛。則何如耶。自去年東北起事以來。遷延已經半載。日本旣唾棄盟約於不顧。而其侵略中國土地之面積。雖西歐任何大國所不及其影響直足牽動世界之和平乃國聯之所以處斷此事。能如昔日之主張公道。絶不爲强權所威脅耶。此事以前之成績且勿論。以後進行。能挽救以前之需滯而追踪解决布希糾紛之光榮耶。

調查團諸公。果欲期「今茲之事件必得圓滿之解决」乎追踪解决布希糾紛之光榮乎。則請一讀最近薛西爾爵士之儻言。爵士之言曰「將來之解决。若不懲治不正當之侵略。若不駁斥黷武政策若不保證國聯忠貞之會員獲所被加害之賠償。則將使國際道德受一危害之打擊」薛丁爾爵士之言揆諸大戰後國際之新道德。亦絶非陳義過高之論。布希糾紛。已成先例。我人惟希望調查團勉力爲之。以自全其信義而已。李頓爵士昨在酬答大學代表詞中。希望「會員國維持和平。心口一致。」今觀安達代表之言如彼。而日本自身之行爲則如此。然則如日本者。非心口最不一致之國家乎。常此心口不一致。則調查團工作之前途。荆棘多矣。我人又不能不爲國聯前途憂。

A Precedent for the Success of the League of Nations Investigation Team

Some days ago, Sir Lytton attended a banquet hosted by Guo Taiqi, the Vice Minister of Foreign Affairs. During the banquet, he remarked, "The League of Nations has been established for ten years. It has encountered crises more than once, but it has always managed to eliminate them without failure. Considering the past achievements, I deeply believe that the current incident will be resolved satisfactorily." These few words overflowed with confidence and a sense of responsibility. What more can we say besides hoping that he fulfills his responsibilities and completes his self-assurance? When we consider what Sir Lytton referred to as past achievements, the most notable is probably the incident of the Bush dispute in 1925.

It is recorded that on October 22, 1925, the Greek army launched a large-scale invasion of Bulgaria, occupying Bulgaria's border. The Bulgarian government urgently telegraphed Geneva, requesting the convening of the Council, citing Articles 10 and 11 of the Covenant. The first step taken by the Council after its meeting was to find ways to stop the hostile acts. The second step was to order Greece to withdraw its troops. The results were all as the League of Nations wished. At that time, Chairman Briand's mediation was particularly diligent, severely condemning countries that violated the Covenant, using the name of self-defense to carry out aggression. The Council also resolved to organize an investigation team, with its tasks roughly divided into three points: first, to investigate the facts and determine responsibility; second, to stipulate compensation; and third, to suggest methods for resolving the dispute. Briand believed that this incident could follow the proper path of the League of Nations and proceed smoothly, and he considered it a model. British Foreign Secretary Chamberlain further believed that this incident could serve as a model for any member state. At that time, the first to support Chamberlain's statement was the Japanese representative, Adachi.

The result of the investigation team's work confirmed that Greece's occupation of Bulgarian territory was a violation of the Covenant. No excuse could cover up this act of aggression, and the responsibility for compensation rested with Greece. Looking at the whole case, from beginning to end, it was based on the Covenant and resolved quickly. The work of the investigation team was very effective. When the investigation team completed its work, the Council specifically organized a review committee, and the chairman was the Japanese representative, Adachi. His review report was particularly righteous and stern, stating: "Invading the land of a neighboring country, even if forced by some circumstances, cannot cover up the fact of violating the agreement and attempting to evade the responsibility for compensation. We believe that this is the highest principle and must be approved by the entire Council." This is truly one of the most glorious pages in the League of Nations' resolution of disputes. At that time, not only did Briand and his colleagues uphold justice and refuse to yield, but the Japanese representative also knew the dignity of the League of Nations Charter and had an appearance of inviolability. But looking back at today's Sino-Japanese dispute, what is it like? Since the incident in the Northeast last year, it has been delayed for half a year. Japan has abandoned the Covenant, and the area of its aggression against Chinese territory exceeds that of any major Western European country. Its influence is enough to affect world peace. Can the League of Nations adjudicate this matter with the same adherence to fairness as in the past, and not be threatened by powerful countries? Let's not talk about the achievements before this matter. Can the future progress save the previous stagnation and follow the glory of resolving the Bush dispute?

If the members of the investigation team truly want to "resolve the current incident satisfactorily" and follow the glory of resolving the Bush dispute, then please read the recent candid words of Sir Cecil. Sir Cecil said: "If the future resolution does not punish improper aggression, does not refute militaristic policies, and does not guarantee that loyal members of the League of Nations receive compensation for the harm they have suffered, it will inflict a damaging blow on international morality." Sir Cecil's words, assessed against the new international morality after the Great War, are by no means an overstatement. The Bush dispute has become a precedent. We only hope that the investigation team will work hard to preserve its credibility. In his response to the representatives of the universities yesterday, Sir Lytton hoped that "member states would maintain peace with consistency between their words and deeds." Now, considering the words of Representative Adachi and the actions of Japan itself, isn't Japan the country with the most inconsistency between its words and deeds? If this inconsistency between words and deeds continues, the future of the investigation team's work will be full of thorns. We cannot help but worry about the future of the League of Nations.
