前途黯淡之停戰撤兵會議彬

上海中日停戰撤兵會議。自三月廿一日末次預備會議决定三項原則。進而自三月廿四日起舉行正式會議。迄今於四月四日集議十一次。停戰協定新草案正文五條。業已通過四條。惟第三條日軍撤退程序。雙方爭持不决。該條中「日軍退駐地點」一點。亦已由軍事小組委員會决定。引翔港江灣萬國體育塲兩處無異議。吳淞鎭與閘北二處則尙在爭持中。另一點「撤兵期限。」乃爲目前爭持之最大關鍵。雖大會今日又將繼續舉行。但此點如不能獲得合理之解决。會議前途。仍極爲黯淡。旬餘來之正式談判。或仍將付諸東流。

吾人廻溯停戰撤兵會議之產生。蓋爲依據於二月廿九日國聯理事會。及三月四日國聯大會兩决議案中「日軍撤退」之主要原則。從可知日方今日之百般刁難。。延不確定撤兵日期。且絕對無撤兵之誠意。爲根本違反國聯之决議

自會議開始至今。我方已一讓再讓。委曲求全。日方一面公表其在滬有兵五萬。戰馬五千。大小砲百五十尊。飛機五十架。以誇耀其軍的。另一面復積極備戰。且在前線挑釁。而我方公表。則不曰「雙方意見接近。」即曰「續有進展。」曰「友邦又有折中辦法。」嗚呼。此將何以對在嘉定太倉崑山等地犧牲身家性命財產擁護國軍作戰。損失十四萬萬元之上海一帶之人民。更何以對無數忠勇將士衛國自救之熱血。而來日之艱難與危機。足以令吾人焦慮者正多。吾人誠不解我政府於開始談判之先。固亦曾一體認日方意旨之所在否也。

日方之主張蓋分停戰撤兵爲兩事。停戰之條件必須我方承認取締排日運動保障僑民之安全。并組織共同委員會監視撤兵區域之行政事宜。觀乎三月三十一新聯社所傳日本軍部之態度。有謂「閘北江灣吳淞之綫。在用兵策略上絕對不能讓步若未得圓桌會議協定之前。便撤至租界毘連地點。是全然與放棄確保上海和平之出兵目的相等。故此點斷不能讓步。即使會議破裂。亦無可如何」之表示。與日荒木陸相「非成立停戰協定。舉行圓桌會議。不能達到出兵目的。故在其成立前。决不撤兵」之主張。即可知日方除非滿足其政治之要求。决無撤兵之誠意。吾人今且不言在撤兵之前開始和平之談判。爲辱國城下之盟。而在日方無誠意撤兵與故意刁難之下。委曲求全之城下之盟。又果能獲得若何之結局耶。

吾人處於當前情勢之下。默察會議前途。暗礁正多。從可知以公理殉和平。和平終於無以確立。吾人對於停戰撤兵會議。今敢再以嚴正之態度。表示吾人之主張

一。不承認日本有條件撤兵。併軍事問題與政治問題爲一談。

二。上海事件與東北事件。不容分别談判。

上列兩項原則。爲我人民最低限度之要求。深盼我政府予以審愼之注意。愼勿忽視其當前艱鉅之重責。

— 王遞常

上尉姓孝脫氏。名勞勃脫。美利堅華盛頓州泰科瑪鄉人也。爲人肫摯而鏠俠。習航空有聲。任職於其國蓋爾飛車（飛機歐西人又名飛車飛車之名甚古我國湯時已有之見帝王世紀）公司。主馭。旋來吾國上海時廢居。（據蓋爾公司蓋爾君言）遂兼任我國航空敎士。與吾國之習航空者多契。今歲日侵上海。見其殘虐無人理。義憤之色。時流溢於眉宇。二月廿一日。忽變服至吳淞。狙擊日飛機。碎其一。二十三日。聞敵飛炸蘇州之耗。立馭一飛機曰波林者。躡其後。遇飛機六。方肆虐於吾蘇民。敵見上尉至。遽上下遮迣而萃攻之。上尉知無幸。然猶從容力禦碎其一機。殲厥渠魁。（據大陸報及泰晤士報）卒以殉。年二十有七。（據上尉致蓋爾公司之文告）耗聞。我國人皆震悼。國議追贈上尉。以軍禮葬於吾國土。異數也王　常曰。我聞方事之殷。上尉語所親曰。我中立國人也。固無所袒於其間。第人類而見非人類之殘殺。我安能忍。我安能忍。我爲正誼人道計。不容與兩立矣。縱死。亦心所甘。我何憾。然未死。愼勿聲。死則報我父母耳。（據二月二十五日時事新報夕刋）嗚呼。上尉今竟以正誼人道死。上尉其無憾矣。然今之非人類之殘殺。猶日肆而無已。上尉其終能無憾乎。上尉以異國人而不忍我國人蒙非人類之殘殺。扶義而起終至投死而無憾。以視同踐國之土。同食國之毛。獨熟視其國之人宰割呼號而無覩。或竟遠走高飛心自保者。則上尉之死。有似乎不智。上尉其果能無憾乎。嗚呼。

— 淸癯

古之賢者。言治民猶治目。治兵猶治齒。治目者務養其目。不得着以塵屑。治齒者務剔其齒。不許容其積垢。嗚嘑。今之治民者。皆塵悄蔽其目。而治兵者皆積垢滿其口。故一任敵宼之蹂躪國境。坐視民衆之塗炭。而曾不稍動於其心。是豈吾人所料哉。

The Dim Future of the Ceasefire and Troop Withdrawal Conference in Shanghai

The Sino-Japanese Ceasefire and Troop Withdrawal Conference in Shanghai. Since the final preparatory meeting on March 21st established three principles, progressing to the official meeting held from March 24th, there have been eleven meetings as of April 4th. Of the five articles in the new draft ceasefire agreement, four have already been passed. However, Article 3, concerning the procedure for Japanese troop withdrawal, remains a point of contention between the two sides. The "location of Japanese troop redeployment" within this article has also been decided by the Military Subcommittee, with no objections to the Yinxiang Port and Jiangwan International Recreation Ground locations. Wusong Town and Zhabei, however, remain under dispute. Another point, "the deadline for troop withdrawal," is currently the biggest key to the contention. Although the general meeting will continue today, if this point cannot be resolved reasonably, the future of the conference remains extremely bleak. The formal negotiations of the past ten days or so may still come to naught.

When we look back at the genesis of the Ceasefire and Troop Withdrawal Conference, it was based on the main principle of "Japanese troop withdrawal" in the resolutions of the League of Nations Council on February 29th and the League of Nations General Assembly on March 4th. From this, it can be seen that the Japanese side's various acts of obstruction today, delaying the determination of a troop withdrawal date, and the absolute lack of sincerity for troop withdrawal, are fundamentally in violation of the League of Nations' resolution.

Since the conference began, our side has yielded and compromised again and again, seeking complete accommodation. The Japanese side, on the one hand, publicly announced that it has 50,000 troops, 5,000 war horses, 150 large and small cannons, and 50 aircraft in Shanghai to boast of its military might. On the other hand, it is actively preparing for war and provoking on the front lines. Our side, when making public announcements, says nothing but "opinions are close between the two sides," or "continued progress is being made," or "friendly countries have new compromise measures." Alas! How can this be justified to the people of the Shanghai area, who have sacrificed their lives, property, and possessions in Jiading, Taicang, Kunshan, and other places to support the national army in combat, suffering losses of 140 million yuan? How can this be justified to the countless loyal and brave soldiers who shed their blood to defend the country and save themselves? The difficulties and crises of the future are enough to make us anxious, and there are many of them. We truly do not understand whether our government had a full understanding of the Japanese side's intentions before the start of negotiations.

The Japanese side's position is to separate the ceasefire from the troop withdrawal into two separate matters. The conditions for a ceasefire require our side to acknowledge the suppression of anti-Japanese movements, guarantee the safety of overseas Chinese, and organize a joint committee to monitor the administrative affairs of the withdrawal area. Looking at the attitude of the Japanese military, as reported by the Xinlian News Agency on March 31st, it said, "The line of Zhabei, Jiangwan, and Wusong is absolutely non-negotiable in terms of military strategy. If we withdraw to a location adjacent to the concessions before an agreement is reached in the round table conference, it is entirely equivalent to abandoning the purpose of dispatching troops to ensure peace in Shanghai. Therefore, this point is absolutely non-negotiable. Even if the conference breaks down, there is no other way." Coupled with the claim by Japanese Minister of the Army Araki that "We cannot achieve the purpose of dispatching troops unless a ceasefire agreement is established and a round table conference is held. Therefore, we will never withdraw troops before it is established." It can be seen that the Japanese side has absolutely no sincerity in withdrawing troops unless it satisfies its political demands. We will now not even discuss the fact that starting peace negotiations before the troop withdrawal is a humiliating treaty of surrender. But under the circumstances of the Japanese side's lack of sincerity in withdrawing troops and deliberate obstruction, what kind of outcome can a treaty of surrender achieved through complete accommodation really achieve?

Under the current situation, silently observing the future of the conference, there are many hidden reefs. From this, we can see that using justice to seek peace, peace will ultimately not be established. Regarding the Ceasefire and Troop Withdrawal Conference, we now dare to express our views again with a serious attitude:

1. Do not recognize conditional troop withdrawal by Japan, and discuss military issues and political issues together.

2. The Shanghai Incident and the Northeast Incident must not be negotiated separately.

The above two principles are the minimum demands of our people. We deeply hope that our government will pay careful attention to them and not neglect its important and arduous responsibilities at present.

Wireless Cinema

— TW

In recent years, scientific progress has been rapid, almost at a pace of a thousand miles a day. What were once astonishing ideals have become everyday realities. To soar through the air and ride the clouds, we have airplanes; to transmit messages across thousands of miles, we have the telegraph; to converse with people in distant places, we have the telephone. Now, we even have the ability to shrink distances and see through obstacles. We can not only hear voices and music from afar, but also sit at home and watch scenes and people from distant lands. Who can say that the "thousand-mile eye" is impossible to realize?

Wireless cinema (Television) is a method of transmitting moving scenes from one place to another using electricity. After the invention of aviation, this is the greatest new invention in the scientific world. In 1909, the American Hugo Gernsback was the first to use the word "Teleusion". For more than twenty years, scientists around the world have been engaged in the research of wireless cinema. Those who have successfully conducted the research include scientists at Bell Telephone Laboratories in the United States, and the Englishman Baird. They have all created effective wireless cinema transmitters and receivers. The principles and functions of these two devices are briefly described below.

The transmission and reception of wireless telephone work by first converting sound waves into electrical waves at the transmitting station, which then pass through the ether of space to the receiver, where the electrical waves are converted back into sound waves. The transmission and reception of wireless cinema are similar. First, light waves emitted from a human face or object are converted into electrical waves at the transmitting station, which are transmitted through space to the receiving device, where the electrical waves are converted back into light waves. The most important components in wireless cinema are the photoelectric cell, which converts light waves into electrical waves, and the neon tube, which converts electrical waves into light waves. The former is known as the "electric eye" and is a highly sensitive photoelectric cell. When light shines on it, it is converted into an electric current. The stronger the light, the stronger the current; conversely, the weaker the light, the weaker the current. If there is no light, there is no current. The latter is a tube filled with neon gas. When an electric current passes through it, it emits light. The stronger the current, the stronger the light emitted; the weaker the current, the weaker the light. If there is no current, it is dark and lightless.

However, with only the above-mentioned transmission method, the image obtained by the receiving device would be nothing more than a blur. This is because the light is merged together. To solve this problem, a light-interrupter is needed. The light-interrupter used in Bell's wireless cinema transmitter and receiver is a rotating perforated disc. The disc has fifty holes arranged in a spiral pattern. When transmitting an image, the human face or object to be transmitted is first illuminated with a strong light, and the rotating disc is rapidly rotated. The light rays falling on the human face pass through the holes in the rotating disc in sequence—that is, the first light ray passes through the first hole, the second light ray passes through the second hole, and so on—and are then projected onto the photoelectric cell, where they are converted into electrical currents of varying strengths. However, these currents are too weak to be used for transmission, so they are amplified by an amplifier and then transmitted through the antenna into space, where they are carried to the antenna of the receiving device and guided to the receiver. An amplifier amplifies the received current and transmits it to the neon tube. This tube emits light of corresponding intensity according to the strength of the current. The light then passes through a rotating disc with holes, the size, position, and rotation speed of which are exactly the same as the disc at the transmitting station. The equal speed of the two discs is controlled by a synchronous motor. The light rays pass through the rotating disc in sequence and are finally projected onto the screen, forming the image of the original object.

This method is tantamount to dividing the human face into two thousand five hundred small squares (fifty in each direction), forming light spots of varying intensities that are scattered and then reassembled at the receiving device, restoring the original appearance. Because the scattering and assembly are extremely rapid, and because the human eye has the effect of persistence of vision, we cannot distinguish the sequence of the spots, but only perceive that the whole image appears simultaneously. Baird's transmitter and receiver are similar to this, except that Baird uses sixteen lenses instead of fifty small holes, and a toothed disc as a light-interrupter.
