告今日從事政治運動者㈡彬· 1

關於舊軍閥官僚政客之企圖。乘機捲土重來。而積極從事於所謂政治運動。昨評所言。猶有未盡。茲請更進一步之說明。

彼舊軍閥官僚政客之所以積極從事政治運動者。其意不謂國民黨主政六年。而國勢乃每况愈下以今例昔。彼輩尙未遂斷絕其政治生命乎。吾人固不願絕其自新之路。但今日之時代。已非前日之時代。政治但當推進入新的軌道。絕不容開倒退之車。彼輩今日之主張則未見較現政府有若何之進步。是則彼輩之活動。不過爲個人之利益耳。即使其能重登政治舞台。而謂其遂能奠定我國家拯救我人民其誰信之。

在此時期。更有一般赤心誠意之國民。怵於國亡之無日。旦夕惶惶。奔走追呼。無以自主。吾人亦不能無一言。

吾人認爲國危民困至於今日之情勢。稍有血氣之倫。皆當以救國救民族爲前提。執政之國民黨。固應虛懐悔禍。毋再固執成見。即暫時不能遽然還政於民。但最小限度亦應自動公開政治。而一般赤心誠意救國之人士。其苦心孤詣。奔走追呼。積極活動。欲以改善政治。固最足欽佩。但欲使力不枉費。心不徒勞。則此處亦有急待解答之先决問題在。

㈠假如國民黨此時而果眞還政於民熱心救國之個人。或主張不同之若干人。果能立即鳥合。以共負國家之大政否。如其不能。則從可知任何有辦法有血氣有毅力之個人。皆無力以挽救當前之難局。必須有嚴密之組織。而此組織。又非以個人爲中心共同奮鬥。乃能有成。

㈡旣有組織矣但此組織如無偉大之生命。則仍無力以爲國也偉大生命者何。即主義是。中山先生手定之三民主義。吾人未敢認其爲不合於我人民之需要。徒以國民黨底定全國。軍閥政治與官僚政治復興。致主義未得實行。是則今日豈尙有較三民主義更適當之主義乎。吾人認爲今日而言挽救民族。必審目前之病徵與病因。以確立具體之方案。不可偏重理想。不可違背時代之潮流。更不容違反民族與大衆之利益。如無主義以爲組織之生命。則組織將毫無所用。此又吾人所不得不認淸者也。

㈢有主義矣。有組織矣。然而我組織中有若德之白魯寗。意之莫索里尼。蘇聨之史逹林之領袖人材乎。有能共信互信之健全幹部乎。有能難苦奮鬥抱犧牲决心之黨員乎。如其無之則吾人之組織。仍爲徒然。吾人之目的。仍無由而逹到。

凡此問題。吾人皆須先有審愼之思考。以談政治則易。以言組織政黨由難。以言創造爲民族爲大衆而奮鬥之政黨則更難。民國以來之共和黨憲政黨及安福系交通系。今日安在耶。若以爲三人集。合即可組黨。即可狂言改造政治。奠定國基。是殆爲自欺欺人。爲鳥托邦之幻夢。吾見其愚狂矣。未見其可也。國民黨亦有其爲民族奮鬥如黃花崗役之光榮歷史。彼惶惶奔走之諸君子。曷其三思之。

An Admonition to Today's Political Activists (II) – Bin 1

Regarding the attempts of former warlords, officials, and politicians to seize opportunities and stage a comeback by actively engaging in so-called political movements, what was said in yesterday's commentary remains incomplete. I now offer a more in-depth explanation.

The reason these former warlords, officials, and politicians are actively engaging in political movements is because they believe that after six years of Nationalist Party rule, the country's situation has only worsened. Using the past as an example, have these people not completely severed their political lives? We certainly do not wish to cut off their path to reform, but today's era is not the same as yesterday's. Politics should only advance onto new tracks and must never allow for regression. The proposals of these individuals today show no progress compared to the current government. Therefore, their activities are merely for personal gain. Even if they could return to the political stage, who would believe that they could establish a foundation for our nation and save our people?

During this period, there are also sincere and earnest citizens who, alarmed by the imminent demise of the nation, are anxious day and night, running around calling for help, unable to control themselves. We cannot remain silent.

We believe that the nation's peril and the people's suffering have reached today's state, and anyone with a sense of patriotism should prioritize saving the nation and the people. The ruling Nationalist Party should sincerely regret its mistakes and not stubbornly cling to preconceived notions. Even if it cannot immediately return power to the people, it should, at the very least, voluntarily make its politics transparent. The sincere and earnest people who are dedicated to saving the nation, who painstakingly exert themselves, running around calling for help, and actively working to improve politics, are truly admirable. However, to ensure that their efforts are not wasted and their hearts do not labor in vain, there are urgent and essential preliminary questions that need to be answered here.

(1) If the Nationalist Party were to genuinely return power to the people at this time, could patriotic individuals or groups with differing opinions immediately unite to jointly bear the burden of national governance? If not, then it can be understood that any capable, passionate, and determined individual is powerless to salvage the current crisis. A strict organization is necessary, and this organization can only succeed if it is not centered on individuals but rather on collective struggle.

(2) Even with an organization, if that organization lacks a great life force, it will still be powerless to serve the nation. What is this great life force? It is the doctrine. The Three Principles of the People, personally established by Mr. Sun Yat-sen, we dare not say are unsuited to the needs of our people. It is merely that after the Nationalist Party unified the country, warlord politics and bureaucratic politics revived, preventing the doctrine from being implemented. Therefore, is there a more appropriate doctrine today than the Three Principles of the People? We believe that to save the nation today, we must carefully examine the current symptoms and causes of the disease to establish concrete solutions. We must not overemphasize ideals, go against the trends of the times, or violate the interests of the nation and the masses. If there is no doctrine to serve as the life force of the organization, then the organization will be useless. This is another point that we must recognize clearly.

(3) Even with a doctrine and an organization, do our organizations have leaders like Germany's Bismarck, Italy's Mussolini, or the Soviet Union's Stalin? Do we have sound cadres who can trust each other? Do we have party members who can struggle through hardship and have the determination to sacrifice? If not, then our organizations will still be in vain. Our goals will still be unattainable.

We must carefully consider all these questions. It is easy to talk about politics, but difficult to organize a political party. It is even more difficult to create a political party that strives for the nation and the masses. Where are the Republican Party, the Constitutional Party, and the Anfu Clique's Communications Clique from the Republic of China era today? If one thinks that gathering three people is enough to form a party and that one can then make wild claims of reforming politics and establishing the nation's foundation, this is merely deceiving oneself and indulging in a utopian fantasy. We see their foolishness and madness, but we do not see their potential for success. The Nationalist Party also has a glorious history of striving for the nation, such as the Huanghuagang Uprising. Let those gentlemen who are anxiously running around give it some serious thought.

Ceasefire Conference Nearly Breaks Down 2

Yesterday's two meetings discussed the troop withdrawal procedures. The Japanese side insisted on withdrawing to the Zhenru-Shizi Lin line. Our representatives firmly opposed and prepared to withdraw. Allied envoys intervened. The meeting will continue this morning. The formal ceasefire conference, attended by representatives from China, Japan, and relevant countries, was held twice yesterday at the British Consulate General to discuss the most crucial Article 2 of the draft ceasefire agreement regarding the Japanese troop withdrawal procedures. The Japanese side insisted on withdrawing to the Zhenru-Shizi Lin line. The Chinese side firmly opposed and expressed its intention to withdraw. After persuasion from allied envoys, it was decided to continue the meeting at 10 a.m. this morning.

Same Representatives Attending 3

Yesterday's meeting was held twice. The first session was from 10:00 a.m. to 12:50 p.m., and the second session was from 3:00 p.m. to 6:50 p.m. The Chinese side was represented by Chief Representative Guo Taiqi, Vice Minister of Foreign Affairs; Military Representative Dai Ji, Garrison Commander of Songhu; and Huang Qiang, Chief of Staff of the 19th Route Army. The Japanese side was represented by Chief Representative Uetda Kenkichi, Commander of the 9th Division; Chief of Staff of the Army, Tashiro Kanichirō; and Chief of Staff of the Navy, Shimada Shigetarō. The allied countries were represented by British Minister Lampson; American Minister Johnson; Italian Chargé d'affaires Ciano; and French Minister Wilden, still represented by Bodard, Counselor of the French Embassy. Chinese and Japanese interpreters and attendants were also present, with the seating arrangement remaining the same as the previous day.

Tense Atmosphere at the Meeting 4

During this Easter holiday, Westerners are on vacation. However, the allied envoys unanimously attended yesterday's ceasefire negotiations and actively participated, which is evident. The preamble and Article 1 of the ceasefire draft agreement, regarding the temporary stationing of our troops on the current defense line, were decided at the previous day's meeting. Yesterday's meeting proceeded to discuss Article 2 regarding the Japanese troop withdrawal procedures. The announcement from the meeting was quite simple. This reporter, after much probing from various diplomatic circles last night, learned that during yesterday's discussion of Article 2 of the ceasefire draft agreement, "Due to the large number of Japanese troops, a portion may be temporarily stationed in the Public Concession and areas adjacent to roads built by the concession beyond the Hongkou area," the Japanese Chief Representative Ueda stood up and stated that the Japanese troops should withdraw to a defense line from Shizi Lin via Yanghang and Dachang in the east to Zhenru in the west. The Chinese representatives believed that this proposal was vastly different from the principles of Japanese troop withdrawal agreed upon by representatives of both sides at the last preparatory meeting on March 21st, and the text and spirit of the League of Nations Council's resolution of March 4th. Therefore, the Chinese representatives immediately expressed their intention to withdraw from the meeting. The atmosphere in the meeting suddenly became tense. Subsequently, after strenuous persuasion from allied envoys such as Britain and the United States, and the Japanese representatives, knowing that their argument was unreasonable, also expressed that they would reconsider. It was then decided that the meeting would resume at 10 a.m. this morning. American Minister Johnson, who initially planned to accompany his wife to Hangzhou for a spring outing with the League of Nations Inquiry Commission this morning, also canceled his plans.

Reuters Correction Telegram 5

Reuters' March 25th correction telegram states: The previous Geneva dispatch reported that during the League of Nations Council meeting on March 16th, Chairman Hymans read the original Chinese telegram, which stated that three measures were drafted at a tea party hosted by British Minister Lampson, the second of which was that Japanese troops would retreat into the Public Concession and out of the roads outside the Hongkou area concession, but because of the large number of Japanese troops, a portion could be temporarily stationed in areas adjacent to the aforementioned locations. Now, this agency has received a verified telegram from Geneva stating that the clause should read: "Japanese troops will retreat to the Public Concession and the roads outside the Hongkou area concession, but because of the large number of Japanese troops, a portion may be temporarily stationed in areas adjacent to the aforementioned area." This correction is made for clarity.

Focus of This Morning's Meeting 6

During the two meetings yesterday morning and afternoon, the Chinese military representative Huang Qiang and the Japanese military representative Tashiro left the meeting room several times to review maps in a separate room. For the sake of ending the matter peacefully and promoting peace, our troops agreed to temporarily station themselves on the current defense line, but the Japanese troops must also completely withdraw to their original positions before January 28th according to a certain procedure. The Japanese side had long advocated withdrawing to the area east of Wusong and the Songhu Road, but it had not been formally proposed until yesterday, when this demand was officially made, and it was even escalated to insist on withdrawing to the line of Zhenru, Dachang, Yanghang, and Shizi Lin. According to those familiar with Japanese-style diplomacy, the true intention of Japan is nothing more than to acquire the area east of Songhu Road as a Japanese troop withdrawal area, as leverage for future round-table conferences. The current insistence on withdrawing to the Zhenru-Shizi Lin line may be a probing tactic, combined with bargaining. Whether or not this morning's meeting can avoid a breakdown depends on this.

Announcement from Last Night's Meeting 7

After last night's meeting, both sides issued a simple statement saying that the meeting had made progress on discussing minor points and that they were now discussing the fundamental points and were trying their best to break through the difficulties, and that the meeting would continue tomorrow morning.

Guo Taiqi's Statement 8

Our Chief Representative Vice Minister Guo issued a statement yesterday, saying that since the start of the ceasefire negotiations, he has repeatedly and clearly stated that the scope of these negotiations is limited to a practical ceasefire between China and Japan and the withdrawal of Japanese troops, and does not involve any political issues. The ceasefire conference and the peace conference are different in nature. The former belongs to military affairs, and the latter belongs to politics. Before the Japanese troops withdraw, we cannot negotiate any political issues under the threat of their military force. Therefore, initially (on the 6th of this month), when he accepted the invitation to a tea party by the British Minister and met with Minister Shigemitsu at the British Consulate to informally exchange opinions, the latter mentioned the issue of Mayor Wu's reply on January 28th. Subsequently, the Special Committee of the League of Nations Council, based on Representative Yan's report, clarified the nature of the Shanghai ceasefire conference, stating that political conditions could not be discussed. Our current ceasefire conference has consistently been based on the League of Nations' repeated resolutions, with the scope limited to military affairs and not discussing issues of a political nature. We have held this purpose and have not dared to change it. However, there have been many speculations from outside, and some have even adopted the propaganda materials from the Japanese side as the basis for their conjectures, and others have described the details of the meetings as secret diplomacy because the details of the talks have not been disclosed.
