Slap and Laugh (Random Reading Thoughts)

— In addition to idleness

Although clowns do not occupy a very important position on the stage, they are always welcomed by the audience - because they can make the audience laugh heartily and relieve boredom. Clowns have done their best in this respect.

Clowns have many ways to get people to laugh. Pressing a piece of white on the nose and shaking a fan while saying humorous words is one way. Climbing walls and jumping on trains, like Rock, doing funny actions is also a way. But the principle seems to be only one, that is, the clown has to say unreasonable words and do unreasonable actions.

But saying humorous words requires turning your mind, and doing funny actions requires effort. For the Russian mystic symbolist, Andreyeve, in his famous play "The Slap in the Face," provided a simple way: a clown does not need any martial arts or literary talent. He just needs to stick out his face and let the other characters on the stage slap him. After the right ear is slapped, let others slap the left face. After being slapped, he has to laugh and bow to the slapper. Once, this clown was slapped a hundred times, which made the audience feel satisfied and laugh out loud, and this clown became popular.

However, if this clown suddenly starts crying sadly because of his own miserable life experience and complains about his intolerable life after stepping down from the stage, then the audience will definitely feel very disappointed. I think someone in the audience will say something like this: "I paid to have fun, not to see you crying."

Audiences do not sympathize with clowns. This is unreasonable. Ancient Roman emperors felt that watching people and beasts fight was a cruel form of entertainment. But I think those who can laugh after watching a clown being slapped are also indulging in a kind of cruel appreciation for entertainment.

However, everyone does not feel this way. After watching the clown being slapped and laughing heartily, if the excitement is still there, perhaps someone will shout, "Another one!" This is probably because people are used to some unreasonable things.

Night Walk

— Zoba

Our class ends at 8 p.m.

She and I are in the same grade and go the same way. Whenever class ends, I always see her rushing back, walking faster than me.

Last night, because of an air-raid drill, the streets were pitch black. As usual, I walked home.

Suddenly, a voice called me from behind, "Zoba, please walk slower. Let's walk together."

I couldn't see who it was, but I heard her clearly.

"Okay," I said, waiting for a few minutes until her footsteps caught up.

During the blackout drill, there were no lights on the road.

"It's not easy to even see people's shadows," she said.

"Oh."

"We should bring a flashlight next time," she added.

"There's no need," I replied. "Unless it's always on, otherwise it will not only blind yourself, but also make others dizzy. We should take this opportunity to train our eyes. Every major city in the world today, such as London, Berlin, Paris, Rome, Moscow, Vienna, etc., is practicing."

"But walking in the dark is always terrible."

"People who walk at night in the countryside can manage it. Moreover, our city has such good, flat, and straight roads. In small places, people walking at night have no street lights, so they look up at the sky. Between the walls on both sides, what they see is the dark, silent sky. If you walk while looking at the sky, you won't bump into anything."

"So you won't stumble in bad places?"

"So it's just for familiar roads that I can answer," I replied.

"Are you saying that people can get used to this kind of darkness?"

"That's right. Everything can be gotten used to. You will soon get used to it, just like a city dweller who is not used to walking long distances. When he arrives in the countryside, where production is abundant and there is no boat or car to take, he has to walk. And after walking more, he will think that he has gained a kind of experience."

Then, the traffic lights shining over the intersection made me feel so comfortable, especially the green light with a longer wavelength, which suddenly gave my tired body a kind of balanced comfort.

"Seeing a light in the darkness seems particularly happy," she said.

"Yes, on nights with bright lights, people don't cherish the lights. Most of the value of the lights is obliterated. At that time, many lights are wasted."

The traffic lights quickly passed, and we ventured in the dark for a long time.

After a few minutes of silence, she said again.

"Yes, it's much better now than when we just came out of the brightly lit classroom. At that time, it wasn't easy to see even people's shadows. Now, you can even see the height of the sidewalks."

In the darkness, I heard her smile, a comforting smile, and it was gentle.

Life and Death

— Yang Zhigang

Recently, newspapers have revealed many noteworthy news items, such as the significant decline in infant birth rates, and in contrast, the unabated surge in the number of abandoned infant corpses. Furthermore, there have been frequent reports from funeral parlors indicating that they are often overcrowded with corpses. These interwoven news stories of life and death are the best reflection of the reality of society.

Among them, the most concerning issue is the drastic decrease in the number of infants born in this city. The decline in birth rates certainly has its realistic and complex objective reasons. However, it is undoubtedly a very serious social problem.

There have been many similar events in modern history. During the First World War and for several years after its end, the infant birth rates in various European countries also experienced a historically rare decline, especially in Britain.

At that time, the Manchester Guardian published a profound commentary stating that blindly hoping for population growth is a barbarian superstition. The question is, are there not countless people who are underfed and poorly clothed, and even more who toil year-round without warmth or nourishment? Adding to this population of unhappy people is of little significance. Therefore, how to find ways to improve the lives of people in this society is, in any case, more important than blindly hoping for an increase in birth rates. At the same time, it is also a prerequisite for addressing the decline in birth rates.

I strongly agree with this and deeply resonate with this famous argument.
