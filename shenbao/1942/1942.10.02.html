The First Night of Blackout Regulations 55

— Zhizhu

It is the first night of blackout regulations. I left my office and headed home. I have been in Shanghai for nearly ten years and have never had dinner at home. From today onwards, I plan to change this habit. People who are busy outside every day should also get up early and go to bed early.

After dinner, the neighborhood head had already gone door-to-door to warn people to cover their lamps and lower their curtains so that no light could be seen outside.

The two children were almost as excited as if it were New Year's.

"Mom, I'll cover the lamps!" the younger one had already climbed onto the table.

"Find the curtains and pull them down!" the older one naturally didn't want to miss the opportunity.

My wife was particularly cautious, running downstairs to the courtyard to look up at the apartment to see if any light was visible outside. I heard there was a fine if you didn't comply, it's no joke. It seemed she had a lot of information.

Looking at the circle of light on the table, I couldn't help but think of the happiness of reading under the oil lamp in my childhood. So I randomly picked up a book from my desk and started flipping through it.

The two children do their homework under the lamp every day, but today they jumped onto the bed. Seeing me flipping through a book, the older one said, "Dad, how can we read in this little light?"

The younger one wasn't as clever as the older one, and she gave herself away first. She continued what her brother said:

"Dad, can we not study today?"

My wife laughed and said, "Those are all good ideas."

I only flipped through two pages of the book and reluctantly closed it. It's rare to come home early, so I should go to bed early.

The landlady, however, ran down from the third floor. She saw me at our door and said with a smile, "Mr. Li, you're home early today too. Go to bed early. The neighborhood head just came to say that the lights have to be turned off at nine o'clock. I was afraid you wouldn't be back. Now that you're back, that's good, hee hee."

"Turn off the lights?" I wondered. Last time, during the air raid drill, many people turned off their lights and went to bed early. Later, I learned that as long as the lights were covered, we could still work as usual. The neighborhood head's words can't be trusted, right?

"No, Mrs. Li knows. This is called a feint. The landlady is used to this kind of thing. There's a fine if you don't turn off the lights. I can't afford to be fined, hee hee. Since Mr. Li is back, go to bed early. I'll go downstairs and tell them, hee hee."

This must be the landlady's idea, I thought. I also heard voices of protest from downstairs, but the landlady's reason was still, "I can't afford to be fined."

Sure enough, at nine o'clock, it felt like we had fallen into hell. Even the circle of light was taken away in the landlady's hee-hee laughter.

"It's going to be ten days," I sighed. But the sound of mahjong in the landlady's room had also stopped. Just sleep in the dark then. When you're fast asleep, you don't know whether it's dark or light.

Fortune and Misfortune 56

— Zixin

Life has become increasingly difficult in recent years, so I often feel resentful. When I see or read advertisements about pampered Pekingese dogs riding in rickshaws or advertisements searching for lost police dogs with rewards, I become even more indignant, feeling a surge of anger.

However, when I accidentally glimpsed a wounded and dying stray dog in a quiet corner, my hatred for dogs suddenly wavered.

Moreover, from this, I had the following feelings:

Perhaps in the past, it was a police dog loyal to its duties, guarding the property of a factory owner, just as young and strong workers worked hard to increase the wealth of the factory owner. When he and it had exhausted their sweat and blood, they were like sugarcane that had been squeezed dry, with only the pulp remaining, and were driven out the door without any mercy.

He and it are both so unfortunate with such unfortunate fates.

But on the other hand, there are people and dogs who reap without sowing. He enjoys the blood and sweat of others, drop by drop, doing nothing, playing with dogs and other things to relieve boredom. It is either exquisite and clever, not needing to work hard, but relying on flattering gestures to eat, drink, and play leisurely, living the life of a pampered darling of adults.

He and it seem to be the lucky darlings of heaven.

Is the fortune and misfortune of people and dogs arranged long ago? Yes, since the beginning of history, mankind has long laid the foundation for fortune and misfortune, but the arrangement is definitely not by some so-called God or Bodhisattva, but precisely by those clever, unearned darlings of heaven who are stabilizing their acquired territories.

From this, I became completely enlightened, and my hatred turned in another direction from then on.

A Nighttime Pilgrimage on the Streets 57

— Ling Zuren

Night brings profound darkness, heavily pressing down on this twentieth-century city.

This place is no longer the city that never sleeps. The red lights that used to paint the half-covered screen with a sunset glow, and the intense headlights of cars that intertwined like the beams of searchlights, have all become relics of its former glory.

Therefore, the streets at night are ruled by darkness, swallowed up by it, filled with an eerie and terrifying atmosphere that replaces the various lights. The only things occupying the streets at night are the red lights atop the self-defense force guard posts, shining like the eyes of a drunkard in the darkness, appearing solemn and stern.

The hearts of pedestrians are gray and heavy. The sound of their footsteps is shrouded in sighs over the ruggedness of life's road.

The barricades at the road junctions, made of bamboo poles, squat in the darkness like ferocious beasts baring their teeth, giving pedestrians a great deal of wariness.

The traffic lights are merely a recollection in the minds of rickshaw pullers. The black uniforms of the Indian police and their two waving white sleeves are indeed a clear and distinct signal.

The sparse lights in a few shops faintly resemble fishing lights on the sea.

The smiles of young people have disappeared from the street corners. Those wanderers who have lost the light are trudging through the darkness in search of their homes.

From someone's radio floats a burst of sad melody. The desolate tune deeply stirs the heartstrings of the wanderers buried in the darkness.

The streets at night in a twentieth-century city are so somber and bleak.
